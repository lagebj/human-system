# Style Guide

**Purpose:** Define the prose style for "Human System" to ensure consistency, clarity, and credibility.

**Last updated:** 2026-06-18
**Status:** Active — revised to prevent mechanical template application

---

## Important: Techniques Are Options, Not Templates

This guide describes techniques that have proven useful. **None are mandatory.**

The prose movement must follow the material, not a template.

**Explicitly allowed:**
- A section without a scene
- A paragraph that does not end with consequence
- A chapter without a named case
- Uneven paragraph lengths that follow thought
- Long, winding sentences where the thinking requires them
- Short paragraphs used deliberately, not as default
- Unresolved endings where the subject remains unresolved

**Explicitly forbidden:**
- Turning any technique into a quota
- Applying techniques mechanically
- Creating a visible pattern through repeated application
- Using a technique because "the guide says so"
- Imitating the examples rather than understanding the principle

## Voice

The voice is:
- **Plain** — simple words for complex ideas
- **Exact** — precise claims, bounded appropriately
- **Calm** — no urgency, no performance
- **Serious** — about the work, not about sounding smart
- **Humane** — about people, not systems as abstractions
- **Non-performative** — no display of intelligence for its own sake
- **Psychologically sharp** — names real dynamics without therapeutic language
- **Grounded in observable work** — concrete before abstract

The reader should often think: *"I knew this, but I had never seen it stated that clearly."*

## Sentence Rules

### One Main Idea Per Sentence

Break sentences that carry several abstract claims.

**Weak:**
> "Organizational transformation often generates identity-based resistance because legacy practices are embedded in informal systems of status, competence, and social legitimacy."

**Better:**
> "Old ways of working carry more than work. They also carry status. They show who knows what. They show who can be trusted."

### Sentence Length

- Mostly short and medium sentences
- Occasional longer sentences when the thought genuinely needs space
- Never choppy or childish
- Never showy or complex for the sake of complexity

**This is a tendency, not a rule.** If a paragraph needs three long sentences to think through something, use three long sentences.

### Active Voice

Prefer active verbs over passive constructions.

**Weak:**
> "The intervention created a redistribution of accountability."

**Better:**
> "After the change, one team carried the blame for errors it could no longer prevent."

## Vocabulary Rules

### Preferred Simple Words

| Use | Instead of |
|-----|------------|
| use | utilize |
| help | facilitate |
| show | demonstrate |
| change | transformation (unless transformation has specific meaning) |
| skill | capability (unless capability means something broader) |
| choice | decision architecture |
| trust | relational capital |
| work | operational activity |
| problem | challenge (when it is a problem) |
| start | commence |
| end | terminate |
| enough | sufficient (unless precision requires it) |

**These are tendencies, not laws.** Use the word that fits. If "utilize" is actually the right word in a specific context, use it.

### Banned Consultant Phrases

Do not use unless the term is essential and clearly defined:

- leverage (as verb)
- unlock
- optimize
- enablement
- transformation journey
- strategic alignment
- stakeholder ecosystem
- human capital
- operating model maturity
- change readiness
- value realization
- thought leadership
- capability uplift
- organizational resilience
- best practice
- low-hanging fruit
- move the needle
- north star
- synergies
- scalable solution
- future-proof
- end-to-end
- cross-functional alignment
- governance framework

### Technical Terms

These terms are central and must be used consistently:

- **human workaround** — informal capability built around broken systems
- **relocated burden** — interventions move burden rather than remove it
- **tacit competence** — knowledge that lives in practice, not documentation
- **technical completion** — the system works
- **organizational completion** — the team can sustain the work
- **expert dependency** — the team waits for the expert rather than building their own capability
- **distributed capability** — capability lives in multiple people, not one
- **resistance as information** — resistance reveals what is being protected
- **power as enacted** — power lives in relationships and actions, not only titles
- **modernization as social adaptation** — not only software delivery

**Use these sparingly.** They should appear when they earn their place, not on a schedule.

### Therapeutic Language

Use carefully. Do not use as loose metaphors for ordinary discomfort.

**Avoid as metaphor:**
- trauma
- trigger
- healing
- safety (use "trust" or "security" when appropriate)
- injury
- nervous system
- harm

**Prefer precise language:**
- distrust
- prior failure
- fear of blame
- loss of status
- uncertainty
- humiliation
- exclusion
- lack of control
- past broken promises

## Argument Rules

### Show the Mechanism

Do not stop at description. Show cause and effect.

**Pattern:**
> They did X because Y. That produced Z.

**Example:**
> "Leaders removed the manual step because it looked wasteful. The team had used that step to catch bad data. The system became faster and less safe."

**This is not mandatory.** Some passages work better as observation without causal chains. Use judgment.

### Bound Claims

Do not make universal claims where the evidence only supports a pattern.

**Weak:**
> "People resist change because they fear losing control."

**Better:**
> "People often resist change when it removes control before it creates trust."

**Use language such as:**
- often
- sometimes
- under these conditions
- in this kind of system
- one explanation is
- this becomes more likely when
- the pattern breaks down when

### Distinguish Nearby Causes

Do not collapse these into one explanation:

- fear of losing status
- loss of real skill
- loss of control
- distrust based on history
- territorial behavior
- misaligned incentives
- ordinary political self-interest
- concern about safety or quality
- discomfort with learning
- resistance to being judged
- practical knowledge that the proposed change ignores

**You do not need to distinguish all of them every time.** Name what matters for the passage at hand.

## Rhythm Rules

### Avoid Repeated Binary Constructions

Use these patterns only when they produce a clear and necessary distinction. Do not let them become the default rhythm.

**Avoid as default:**
- "It is not X. It is Y."
- "Both things are true."
- "The question is not whether. The question is where."
- "On the one hand... on the other hand..."

**Occasional use is fine.** Repeated use becomes mechanical.

### Vary Paragraph Length

- Some paragraphs are one sentence
- Some paragraphs are five sentences
- Most paragraphs are two to four sentences
- Never mechanical uniformity

**Let the thought decide.** If a paragraph needs to be eight sentences, make it eight sentences.

### Use Reversals Sparingly

A good reversal changes how the reader sees the issue.

**Examples:**
> "The workaround was not outside the system. It was part of the system."

> "The old process was slow. It was also where judgment lived."

> "The rollout finished. The change had not."

> "The expert solved the problem. The team learned to wait."

**Do not force a reversal into every paragraph.** One per section is often enough. Zero is sometimes right.

### End Paragraphs with Consequence (Optional)

The last sentence of a paragraph **may**:
- state the consequence
- sharpen the distinction
- carry the argument forward
- reveal the mechanism

**It may also:**
- leave something unresolved
- circle back to an earlier observation
- simply stop when the thought stops

**Weak:**
> "This is where the deeper work begins."

**Better:**
> "The software was ready. The people carrying the risk were not."

**But also acceptable:**
> "She documented this. She set a date for reassessment. The date passed. She was still needed for cascading failures. She accepted this."

## Scene-to-Meaning Movement (Optional Technique)

For important ideas, **consider** this sequence:

1. Show a concrete moment
2. Explain what is happening
3. Name the pattern if a name helps
4. Show the consequence
5. State the leadership implication

**Example:**
> "The manager answered every hard question. The team stopped trying first. His expertise solved the day's problem and became the next day's dependency."

**This is not mandatory.** Some passages work better as direct observation or argument. Do not force every idea into a scene.

---

## The Three-Step Descent (Optional Technique)

A technique for compression:

1. Visible action
2. Hidden meaning
3. System effect

**Example:**
> "The team rebuilt the old spreadsheet.
> It was where they still trusted their own judgment.
> The new platform had replaced the task before the organization replaced the trust."

**Use where it improves clarity.** Do not turn the entire manuscript into fragments.

## Before-and-After Examples

### Example 1: Simple Words

**Before:**
> "The intervention created a redistribution of accountability across the organizational structure."

**After:**
> "After the change, one team carried the blame for errors it could no longer prevent."

### Example 2: Concrete Before Abstract

**Before:**
> "Resistance often reflects a threat to professional identity."

**After:**
> "The team kept the spreadsheet after the new system went live. They called it a backup. It was also where they still knew how to be useful."

### Example 3: Show the Mechanism

**Before:**
> "Modernization often fails due to inadequate attention to social adaptation."

**After:**
> "The deployment system went live two weeks early. The technical work was flawless. Adoption stalled at thirty percent. Teams reverted to the old system. The team had not built the capability to support the new system."

### Example 4: Bound Claims

**Before:**
> "People resist change because they fear losing control."

**After:**
> "People often resist change when it removes control before it creates trust."

### Example 5: End with Consequence

**Before:**
> "This is where the real work of leadership begins."

**After:**
> "The capability was his. She had built it by not doing the work herself."

## Quality Checks

The prose fails if:

- It sounds academic or consultant-like
- Complex ideas are hidden behind abstraction
- Short sentences become choppy or childish
- Every paragraph uses the same rhetorical pattern
- The narrator sounds superior to the people being described
- Universal claims remain unbounded
- Therapeutic terms are used loosely
- Nuance is removed in the name of simplicity
- The prose becomes generic
- Central concepts become inconsistent
- **Any technique from this guide becomes a visible, repeated pattern**

## Inspiration (Not Imitation)

The style draws from these thinkers without imitating their exact voice:

- **Edgar Schein** — careful distinctions and precise definitions
- **Chris Argyris** — expose contradictions between stated intent and actual effect
- **Mary Parker Follett** — use tension and conflict to reveal a better frame
- **Amy Edmondson** — move from scene to concept to practical meaning
- **Karl Weick** — compress complex ideas into clear reversals
- **Robert Senge** — make feedback loops and system effects visible
- **Robert Kegan and Lisa Lahey** — show the hidden logic behind resistance
- **Henry Mintzberg** — contrast official stories with how work actually happens

The final prose must sound like one author, not a mixture of borrowed styles.
