# Human System

*One continuous walk.*

---

**p1** — You arrive somewhere that has been working without you, and the first thing you notice is what has formed around the broken things. People have built ways of carrying the weight that the system was supposed to carry, and they have been carrying it long enough that the weight now feels like part of the work itself. You can see this in how they talk about what they do — the workaround is no longer described as a workaround, it is described as the way the thing works.

**p2** — When you offer to help, you are offering to change what the weight is made of, and this is a different proposition than it sounds like. The people who have been carrying it have developed strength in particular places, and that strength is now part of how they understand themselves as competent. Taking the weight away makes something easier, and it also makes some part of their competence obsolete, and they know this even if they cannot say it in the language of help and improvement. The knowing lives in the body before it lives in words.

**p3** — This is where most interventions begin to fail. The help is understood correctly by the people receiving it, and they understand something that the people offering it often do not, which is that help always changes the system it enters, and the change is not always in the direction that the word "help" suggests. The system was already working. It was working around the problems you see, and it was working through the people who learned to carry what the system could not. When you arrive with a solution, you are arriving in a system that has already solved the problem in the only way it could, which was to distribute the cost across the people who had no choice but to absorb it. Understanding this changes what you think you are doing.

**p4** — Your help will change the system. The question is whether you understand what you are changing when you change it. You are changing where the weight lives. You are changing who carries it. You are changing what competence looks like. And you are doing all of this while using language that suggests you are simply making something work better, as if "working better" is a neutral state that exists independently of the people who have to live inside the system you are improving. The language creates a gap between what you say you are doing and what is actually happening, and people inside the system feel that gap in their bodies before they can name it.

**p5** — I have watched this happen enough times that I no longer believe in the word "enablement" as it is commonly used. Enablement suggests that you are giving someone the ability to do something they could not do before, and what actually happens is that you are relocating where the ability lives. The ability was already there. It was living in the person who stayed late to fix the thing that broke every week. It was living in the team that developed a ritual for handing off work that the tool was supposed to handle. It was living in the judgment of the person who knew which alerts to ignore because the alert system was designed by someone who did not understand what the system actually did. When you "enable" the system, you are moving ability from one place to another, and the move itself has consequences that the word "enablement" does not capture. The consequences arrive later, when the old competence has been made obsolete and the new system has not yet grown its own calluses.

**p6** — The consequences show up later. They show up when the person who used to fix the thing every week no longer knows how to fix it, and the thing breaks in a new way that the new system does not handle. They show up when the ritual for handing off work is forgotten, and the new tool fails in a way that no one knows how to work around. They show up when the alerts are all accurate, and the person who used to read them now has no ground under their feet. What they had was a felt sense of what the alert meant in context, built up through repeated exposure to the system's behavior. They learned to spot patterns in how the alerts arrived, in what else was happening when they fired, in the texture of the noise itself. This is a form of competence that lives in the person, not in the system. When you replace the alert system, you are replacing the ground on which the gut feeling was built. You are building better systems, and you are also building them without understanding what you are replacing, and both things can be true at the same time.

**p7** — The systems we work inside are made of decisions that people made, and those decisions live in the work that people do. When you change the system, you are changing the work. You are changing what people do with their time. You are changing what they pay attention to. You are changing what they are responsible for. And you are changing what happens when the system fails, because every system fails, and the question is always who carries the failure when it does. The failure will come. The question is where it lands.

**p8** — I want to walk through what happens when you enter a system with the intention of changing it. I have watched it happen enough times that I recognize the patterns. The patterns are not about technology. They are about what happens to people when the work they do changes, and the change is described as improvement even when the improvement is experienced as loss. The loss is real. The improvement is also real. Both things can be true at the same time, and the tension between them is where the actual work lives. Walking through this tension requires holding both things without collapsing them into a story that makes the work feel cleaner than it is.

---

**p9** — The charter you receive describes a problem that can be named and solved. It describes a gap between current state and desired state, and it describes your role as closing that gap. The kickoff deck shows a timeline. The status reports show progress against milestones. This is the official story, and it is the story that gets told in rooms where decisions are made about whether the work is succeeding.

**p10** — What is actually happening is different. The system is trying to change without changing itself. The people who work inside it are trying to reach a different outcome while continuing to do the work in the way they have always done it. They want the result without the reorganization of their own time, attention, and responsibility that the result would require. This feels like hypocrisy from the outside, and from the inside it is the natural state of a system that has learned to survive by maintaining its own shape. The system is protecting itself, and the people inside it are protecting themselves, and both protections look like resistance to the person trying to help.

**p11** — There is a history here that does not appear in the charter. People have tried to change this before. There were tools that were adopted and then abandoned. There were reorganizations that moved boxes on the org chart without moving the work. There were processes that were documented and then ignored. There was training that people attended and then forgot. Each attempt left something behind. Each failure taught people something about what happens when you propose change here. The problem has grown formations around itself, like the broken things you noticed on arrival. The formations are made of skepticism, of learned helplessness, of protective cynicism, of the quiet understanding that this too will pass. These formations serve a purpose. They protect people from investing energy in something that will not survive.

**p12** — You can feel the expectation in the first conversations, the ones that happen after the official meetings end. Someone leans in and says, "What we really need is..." and then they tell you what the charter does not say. They tell you about the meeting that cannot happen, the decision that cannot be made, the person who cannot be told. They tell you what would actually need to change for the outcome to be possible, and they tell you that none of it can be named in the room where the work is officially happening. They are giving you the information you need to do the work, in the only way they can give it. The information comes wrapped in caution because the person giving it has learned what happens when this information is spoken too loudly.

**p13** — The information comes in layers. The first layer is the official story, the one that appears in the deck and the status report. The second layer is the real problem, the one that gets shared in confidence. The third layer is the history, the one that explains why the real problem cannot be named. There are more layers beneath these. The political landscape, which you learn by watching who takes risks and who does not. The emotional landscape, which you learn by paying attention to what happens in your own body when you are in certain rooms with certain people. The deck contains none of this. All of it is the material you are actually working with, and you will need to learn to work with material that does not appear in official channels.

**p14** — You occupy a position that is both necessary and suspect. Something needs to change and the people inside the system cannot change it themselves, which makes you necessary. You are the one proposing the change, and the change will require them to become different people than they are right now, which makes you suspect. People want relief from the pain they are carrying, so they welcome you. People know that relief will come with a cost they did not choose, so they resist you. You are given access to information that does not appear in official channels. You are held at a distance from the decisions that would actually use that information. Both things are true. Both things are happening at the same time, and you will need to learn to work inside this tension without trying to resolve it.

**p15** — The people you are working with are responding correctly to the situation they are in. They understand the incentives. They understand the risks. They understand what happens to people who propose certain kinds of change here. They are protecting themselves and each other in the ways that people have learned to protect themselves and each other in this system. Understanding what they understand, and working from that ground instead of the ground you wish you were standing on, is your job. This requires you to set aside judgment and learn to see the situation through their eyes, which is harder than it sounds because you came here to change things and changing things requires you to believe something is wrong.

**p16** — This is slower than the language of improvement suggests. It is messier. It is more human. It requires you to hold multiple truths without collapsing them into a single story that makes the work feel cleaner than it is. The work is not clean. The work is people, and people are not clean. Learning to work with this messiness without trying to clean it up is part of the work itself.

---

**p17** — When you care about helping, something in you responds. The response is not chosen. It rises out of what you are seeing and feeling in the room. You have spent years building a particular kind of attention. That attention notices certain things before it notices others. What it notices first will pull you in a particular direction.

**p18** — Some people see a better way of working before they see anything else. They have watched teams become more coherent when they inhabit a particular approach. They have felt what becomes possible when people share a language, a rhythm, a set of practices. When they enter a struggling group, they see the gap between what is happening and what could happen if the group worked differently. Their body leans toward closing that gap. They begin to imagine what the group could look like if they adopted this approach. The imagination feels like care. It is care. They want to help. The desire to help meets the vision of a better way, and this creates a powerful current. When this current is leading, the group's pain gets interpreted through the lens of the better way. The pain becomes evidence that the way is needed. What can happen is that the way becomes more important than the people it is meant to help. People who have been burned by frameworks, by orthodoxy, by being converted will feel this current as pressure to adopt something they did not choose. They may experience it as rigidity, as superiority, as naivety. They may be right. The way may have real value. The way it lands may still feel like an invasion. The invasion feels real to them even when your intentions are generous.

**p19** — Some people feel pain in the system before they see anything else. They sense tension, incoherence, waste, ambiguity, misalignment, unfairness, avoidable suffering. Something in their body reacts to the gap between what is happening and what seems possible. They feel pulled to resolve the tension, to defend people, to protect the group, to make the situation more coherent. The pull is physical. It lives in the chest, in the stomach, in the tightness that shows up in certain rooms. When this pull is leading, urgency gets felt before consent. Things get named before the group is ready to name them together. Pain gets carried that nobody asked this person to carry. People who dislike emotional pressure, moral intensity, or being implicitly cast as part of the harm will feel this pull as too sensitive, too dramatic, too over-involved, too destabilizing. They may be right. The pain may be real. The way it is carried may still feel like too much. The person carrying it may feel alone in their perception of the pain, and that loneliness can make them carry it harder.

**p20** — Some people see an outcome that matters too much to keep failing at. The cause becomes the center of gravity. Tactics, methods, coalitions, arguments, compromises, operating models get judged by whether they move the outcome forward. The motivating force is the importance of what is at stake, not the elegance of the approach or the felt pain in the room. When this center of gravity is leading, the importance of the outcome becomes permission to override people. The pace becomes faster than relationships can sustain. Context gets treated as secondary to achieving the goal. People who place high value on relationships, local knowledge, fairness, careful process will feel this as pushing too hard, moving too fast, treating people and context as secondary. They may be right. The outcome may matter enormously. The way it is pursued may still feel like a bulldozer. The bulldozer gets you to the outcome and leaves behind damaged relationships that will need to be repaired if the outcome is going to last.

**p21** — Some people see blocked capacity before they see anything else. Their concern is whether people build the power, relationships, confidence, leadership to shape their own future. The work becomes less about personally resolving the tension, installing a method, advancing the outcome through their own effort. The focus becomes helping the group develop shared language, organize around common interests, build relationships, practice leadership, name power dynamics, increase their ability to act. When this concern is leading, respect for self-determination becomes an excuse to withhold needed structure, expertise, protection. Stepping back happens when people need stepping in. Facilitation happens when people need decision. Process gets honored when people need protection. People who want clarity, decisiveness, expertise, relief from ambiguity will feel this as indirect, slow, overly facilitative, unwilling to lead. They may be right. Building capacity may be the goal. The way it is pursued may still feel like abandonment. The person who wants you to lead may experience your restraint as a refusal to use the power you have.

**p22** — These responses attach to who you understand yourself to be. You may take pride in understanding a particular way of working. You may have spent years developing that craft. When someone dismisses the way, it may not feel like they are rejecting an idea. It may feel like they are dismissing something you have worked hard to become good at. You may identify with your ability to sense incoherence, pain, tension in a system. That is a real strength. When people do not see what you see, or seem unwilling to respond to it, it can feel lonely, invalidating, maddening. You may tie part of your self-worth, safety, purpose to the outcome. If the outcome stalls, gets diluted, treated as optional, it may not feel like a normal strategic disagreement. It may feel like something important about you, your values, your reason for being there is being threatened. You may see your strength in your ability to step back, foster capacity, help the group act for itself. When others demand quick answers, decisive direction, heroic leadership, you may feel misunderstood, pulled away from what you believe real help requires. Who you understand yourself to be gives these responses depth, commitment, patience, craft. It also raises the stakes. When a response becomes part of who you are, resistance to the response can feel like resistance to you. A critique of the way can feel like a critique of your expertise. A lack of urgency can feel like a rejection of your values. A leader's dismissal of tension can feel like a dismissal of your perception. A demand for more direction can feel like a rejection of your belief in capacity. That is when it becomes harder to stay curious. Staying curious requires you to separate who you are from what you are proposing, and that separation is difficult when your proposal feels like an extension of yourself.

**p23** — These responses are channeled through your relationship with power. There is the power around you, and there is your own. A person with power may bless the change, block it, ignore it, hijack it, protect it, distort it. They may be the person you need to influence. They may be the source of the problem. They may be a genuine advocate who still struggles to help. They may say they support the work and hesitate when it requires real political capital. When one of your responses meets someone else's power, it can trigger strong feelings. You may feel anger when power is used carelessly. You may feel helpless when people with authority will not act. You may feel fear when the source of power is also the source of threat. You may feel urgency when people are being harmed and the people who could help remain quiet. These experiences can stir feelings that reach back further than this situation. They can touch old experiences with authority, safety, fairness, belonging. That can make these moments extremely hard to navigate. The intensity of your reaction may reflect more than the present situation alone. It may reflect a lifetime of experiences with power that are being activated in this moment.

**p24** — There is your relationship to your own power. You may have more influence than you realize. Credibility, expertise, relationships, access, judgment, language, the ability to make something visible. You may have formal authority. A role, title, budget, team, decision right, the ability to approve, deny, prioritize, protect, delay, expose. That brings up its own feelings, energy, tension. You may underuse your power because you do not want to be coercive, political, self-important, unfair. You may overuse it because the pain feels intolerable, the outcome feels urgent, the better future feels obvious. You may hide your power behind the language of helping. You may deny your power because admitting it would mean admitting responsibility. This can cut deeper than people expect. It is deeply personal. Your relationship to your own power may touch old experiences of responsibility, exclusion, shame, fear, guilt, pride, helplessness. These old experiences will shape how you use power in this situation, often without your conscious awareness.

**p25** — There is another angle. The burden of noticing, translating, smoothing tension, naming harm, educating others, holding the group together is not evenly distributed. The work of making a system more humane often falls disproportionately on people who are already carrying more of the cost. In many organizations, people from underrepresented groups are typed as helpers and asked to do extra emotional, cultural, political labor. They may be expected to make pain legible, soften the message, protect others from discomfort, represent a group, educate colleagues, help the system improve while also absorbing the effects of the current system. That deserves more attention than I can give it here. I am naming it because it shapes what happens when you try to help. The person who is already carrying this burden will experience your help differently than the person who has never been asked to carry it.

**p26** — These responses and power dynamics can create a spiral. Someone feels tension and incoherence. Their body is pulled to name the pain, reduce it, protect people from it. A leader responds with something like, "Well, why didn't the managers deal with that?" That may be a reasonable question. For someone whose body is already pulled toward resolving the tension, the temperature rises quickly. It can feel like the person with more power is avoiding the pain that power helped create, or at least has some responsibility to address. The person trying to help may assume that the people closest to the problem are not in a position to name it publicly. That may be true. They may have said things privately that they do not feel safe saying in the room. They may have real reasons to be cautious. The helper may also misjudge it. They may underestimate the group's capacity, overestimate the danger, start shouldering something the group has not asked them to shoulder. Then, because they want to make progress, they reach for a practice. A better meeting, a clearer operating rhythm, a retrospective, a decision log, a working agreement, a way to surface tensions more safely. That may come from care and experience. It can also trigger someone else's allergy. For the leader, the body-pull toward resolving tension may already feel like emotional pressure, moral intensity, an implicit accusation. Then the pull toward a better way shows up too, because the proposed next step is a practice. Now it can sound like, "And here is the process you should have been using all along." To the leader, it may feel like the helper believes a practice can solve a power problem. It may sound naive, procedural, overly earnest. They may hear, "If we just ran the right ritual, the tension would go away," even if that is not what was meant. If the leader has power, their skepticism may hit the helper harder. It may not feel like ordinary disagreement. It may feel like being dismissed by someone who has the power to dictate reality. If the leader says, "I don't think that's the issue," or "This feels like process for process's sake," the helper may feel reduced to the "process person," even if they are trying to point at something deeper. Now the situation is charged on multiple levels. The helper's body-pull may be allergy-inducing for someone who dislikes emotional pressure or being implicated in harm. Their pull toward a better way may be allergy-inducing for someone who is allergic to frameworks, rituals, being converted. The leader's power may be allergy-inducing for someone sensitive to being dismissed by people with authority. The helper may be reacting to what feels like power deflecting responsibility. The leader may be reacting to what feels like someone reducing a messy leadership situation to a framework choice. The helper may be assuming the group cannot safely say publicly what they have said privately. The leader may be assuming the proposal reflects naivety about how change really happens. And on and on it goes. The spiral continues because each person is responding to what they feel is happening, and what they feel is happening is shaped by their own history, their own relationship to power, their own understanding of what help requires.

**p27** — This takes an incredible amount of energy and patience. Not just the work of changing the system. The work of noticing what is getting activated in you, what is getting activated in others, how who you are is getting pulled in, how power is shaping the interaction. As your energy gets depleted, you are more likely to get pulled in. Once you are pulled in, you are more likely to escalate. You may push harder, read resistance less generously, over-identify with the pain, reach too quickly for a practice, turn disappointment into anger, begin to threaten, subtly or directly. When escalation turns into threat, you often diminish your odds of creating the change you were hoping for. The change you were hoping for requires relationships that can sustain tension, and threat damages those relationships.

**p28** — Understanding your relationship to power is critical. That includes your response to other people's power and your relationship to your own. Both can get very personal, very quickly. Power can touch old experiences of authority, fairness, safety, belonging, responsibility, shame, helplessness, being dismissed. Understanding who you understand yourself to be matters too. The response may be connected to something you take pride in. Your craft, your values, your ability to sense tension, your commitment to an outcome, your belief in helping people build capacity. When that gets touched, resistance can feel less like disagreement and more like a rejection of something important about you. This is where self-awareness becomes practical rather than abstract. Knowing what gets activated in you allows you to notice it before it drives your behavior.

**p29** — Each response carries its own consequence. Each can trigger people around you in different ways. The pull toward a better way can sound like conversion. The pull toward resolving pain can sound like accusation. The pull toward an outcome can sound like pressure. The pull toward capacity can sound like ambiguity or abdication. Knowing how your response might land allows you to adjust how you carry it, not to stop carrying it but to carry it in a way that others can actually receive.

**p30** — The point is to notice what caring is doing to you, how it is landing with others, whether you still have the energy and perspective to help. Noticing creates space between the impulse and the action, and in that space you can choose how to respond rather than simply reacting.

**p31** — There is work on helping that captures the paradox. From the inside, the desire to help may feel generous, careful, well-intended. From the outside, it may land as pressure, judgment, superiority, rescue, control. The other person may feel exposed, corrected, diminished, put "one down," even if that was not the intent. Helping requires more than care. It requires attention to the relationship your help is creating. Are you increasing the other person's capacity and agency, or are you making yourself the one who sees, knows, carries, fixes? Are you helping in a way that the other person can actually receive, or are you adding another layer of pressure to an already charged situation? Sincerity is not enough. The impact of your help is determined by how it lands, not by what you intended.

**p32** — Self-care matters. Self-care is the discipline of staying in the right relationship with your own desire to help. If you are someone who notices pain, imagines better futures, feels pulled to intervene, you need ways to conserve your energy and keep your perspective. Otherwise, the very thing that makes you useful can start to work against you. You may become emotionally overinvested, assume action is needed before conditions are right, push harder than others are ready for. Over time, this can lead to frustration, poor judgment, burnout. Self-care, in this context, is learning to stay useful without becoming consumed. It means asking: What is being activated in me right now? How do I personally identify with that activation? What part of me needs this to be seen, valued, vindicated? What power dynamic am I reacting to? Am I helping in a way that others can receive? These questions create pauses in the momentum of helping, and the pauses allow you to check whether your help is actually helping.

**p33** — Caring less is not the goal.

**p34** — The goal is to care with enough clarity, pacing, and humility that your help remains helpful. This requires ongoing attention to what is happening in you, what is happening in others, and what is happening between you. The attention itself is a form of care.

---

**p35** — You have noticed what is broken and what has formed around it. You have understood your own position and what is being activated in you. You have paid attention to the layers of information and the landscape you are walking into. Now you begin to work.

**p36** — The first thing you will notice is that the work does not begin where the charter says it begins. The charter describes a technical problem, a process gap, a capability deficit. The work begins somewhere else. It begins in the conversations that happen before the work is officially underway. It begins in the relationships that need to be formed before any change can land. It begins in the trust that needs to be built before anyone will tell you what is actually happening. These conversations, relationships, and trust-building are not prerequisites for the real work. They are the real work.

**p37** — You will be tempted to start with the solution. The solution is clear to you. You have seen this before. You know what would work. You have a method, a practice, a way of working that has helped other groups in similar situations. Starting with the solution feels like progress. It feels like you are earning your place. It feels like you are demonstrating competence. This temptation is not wrong. It is the pull toward a better way meeting the desire to be useful. The temptation will lead you to propose something before you understand what you are proposing into. Understanding what you are proposing into requires time that feels like it could be spent building the solution, and that tension between understanding and building is where the work lives.

**p38** — You will be tempted to start with the pain. You have heard stories that land in your body. You have seen people struggling. You have felt the tension in the room when certain topics come up. Starting with the pain feels like honesty. It feels like you are naming what everyone already knows. It feels like you are creating permission to talk about what matters. This temptation is not wrong. It is the body-pull toward resolving tension meeting the desire to relieve suffering. The temptation will lead you to name things before the group is ready to name them together. Naming things before the group is ready can create relief for you and discomfort for them, and that mismatch will show up as resistance that you may interpret as them not being ready for change.

**p39** — You will be tempted to start with the mission. The outcome matters. People are being harmed. The cost of inaction is real and measurable. Starting with the mission feels like clarity. It feels like you are keeping everyone focused on what actually matters. It feels like you are preventing the work from getting lost in process and politics. This temptation is not wrong. It is the center of gravity toward outcome meeting the desire to make progress. The temptation will lead you to push harder than the relationships can sustain. The relationships are the medium through which the outcome will be achieved, and damaging them in service of the outcome can make the outcome impossible to reach.

**p40** — You will be tempted to start with the group's agency. They need to own this. They need to build the capacity to sustain the change. They need to develop the relationships and the language and the leadership to carry this forward without you. Starting with agency feels like respect. It feels like you are honoring their ability to act for themselves. It feels like you are avoiding the trap of creating dependency. This temptation is not wrong. It is the concern for capacity meeting the desire to empower. The temptation will lead you to step back when people need you to step in. Stepping back when people need you to step in can feel like abandonment to them, and that feeling will damage the trust you are trying to build.

**p41** — None of these temptations are mistakes. They are signals. They are telling you something about what is being activated in you. The work is to notice the signal without letting it drive. The work is to feel the pull without following it immediately. The work is to stay present with the tension between what you want to do and what the situation needs. This staying-present is uncomfortable because it requires you to hold your own impulse without acting on it, and the impulse was activated because you care and you want to help and not acting feels like not helping.

**p42** — The actual beginning is slower. It looks less like progress. It feels more like waiting. You spend time in conversations that do not have a clear purpose. You listen to stories that you have heard before. You ask questions that seem obvious. You pay attention to who is in the room and who is not. You notice what happens when certain people speak. You notice what happens when certain topics come up. You notice what people do not say. You notice what you feel in your own body when you are in certain spaces. This noticing is the work of building understanding, and understanding is the ground from which any viable intervention will emerge.

**p43** — This is the work. The relationship-building, the trust-building, the understanding-building that feels like preparation is actually the work itself. The interventions you will eventually propose will only land if this work has been done.

**p44** — The relationships you are building are the medium through which change will travel. The trust you are earning is the foundation that the real work will be built on. The understanding you are developing is the ground from which any viable solution will emerge. Treating these as means to an end will damage them, and damaged relationships, trust, and understanding cannot carry the weight of change.

**p45** — You will feel pressure to move faster. The charter has a timeline. The stakeholders have expectations. The pain is real and people want relief. This pressure is real. It is also not the whole picture. The whole picture includes the fact that change will not land until the ground is ready to receive it. The whole picture includes the fact that relationships cannot be rushed without breaking. The whole picture includes the fact that understanding cannot be shortcut without creating solutions that solve the wrong problem. Holding the whole picture while feeling the pressure requires you to tolerate discomfort without resolving it prematurely.

**p46** — You will need to find a way to honor the pressure without being driven by it. You will need to find a way to move at a pace that respects both the urgency and the reality. You will need to find a way to communicate progress that does not lie about where the work actually is. This finding is ongoing. You will not find it once and then have it. You will need to find it again and again as the situation changes.

**p47** — This is where the helper's position becomes most difficult. You are held accountable for outcomes that depend on relationships you cannot control. You are expected to deliver solutions that depend on understanding you are still building. You are asked to move faster than the work can move without breaking. You are judged by timelines that were set before anyone understood what the work would require. The difficulty here is that there is no solution that resolves the tension. The tension is structural. It comes from the nature of the work itself.

**p48** — There is no clean solution to this tension. There is only the work of navigating it with enough honesty that you do not lose yourself in the process. There is only the work of communicating clearly about what is possible and what is not. There is only the work of building relationships that can sustain the pressure without breaking. There is only the work of staying present with the tension without collapsing it into a story that makes it feel more manageable than it is. This work is exhausting. It requires you to give up the comfort of believing there is a clean solution. It requires you to accept that the messiness is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be worked inside.

**p49** — The work is people. The work is relationships. The work is trust. The work is understanding. The work is slow. The work is messy. The work is human. Accepting this means giving up the fantasy that the work could be something else. The work is what it is. Working inside this reality, rather than fighting it, is where your energy becomes available for the actual work instead of being consumed by the fight.

---

**p50** — You have built enough understanding to propose something. The proposal itself will teach you what you still do not understand. People will respond to your proposal in ways that reveal information you could not have accessed any other way. The proposal is not a solution waiting to be approved. The proposal is a probe that reveals the shape of the system by how the system responds to it.

**p51** — The first proposal will not be the one that lands. This is not failure. This is the work of learning where the actual obstacles live. The obstacles are not the ones described in the charter. They are the ones that show up when you propose something that would actually change how people work. Someone will say they support the change and then not make the decision that would enable it. Someone will say the timing is wrong. Someone will say they need more information. Someone will say nothing at all. Each response teaches you something about where the weight actually lives in this system.

**p52** — You will learn to read the responses. The ones who say yes and mean it. The ones who say yes and cannot deliver. The ones who say no and will help you find another way. The ones who say nothing and hold the power to block everything. The ones who say one thing in the room and another thing after the room empties. None of these people are being dishonest. They are navigating their own constraints, their own incentives, their own understanding of what is possible here. Understanding their constraints does not remove the constraints. It gives you information about what it would take to move them.

**p53** — Moving someone requires you to understand what they are protecting. Sometimes they are protecting themselves from risk. Sometimes they are protecting someone else from consequences. Sometimes they are protecting the system from instability. Sometimes they are protecting you from a failure you do not yet see. The protection looks like resistance from where you are standing. From where they are standing, it looks like responsibility. Both perspectives are accurate. The work is to find a path that honors the protection while still moving forward.

**p54** — You will need to find the people who can actually make decisions. This is not always the people with the titles. Sometimes the person who can make the decision is the one who has been here long enough to know what will stick and what will not. Sometimes it is the person who carries the relationships that would be damaged if the decision went the wrong way. Sometimes it is the person who has the credibility to absorb the blame if things go wrong. Finding these people requires you to watch how decisions actually get made, not how they are supposed to get made.

**p55** — Once you find the decision-makers, you will need to understand what they need to feel safe making the decision. Safety is not about eliminating risk. Safety is about having enough cover, enough support, enough evidence that the decision will not cost them more than they can afford. The evidence they need is not always data. Sometimes it is the visible support of other people they trust. Sometimes it is a pilot small enough that failure would not be noticed. Sometimes it is simply time, the time to let the idea circulate until it feels like it came from them.

**p56** — You will be tempted to claim credit for ideas that need to come from someone else. This temptation is the desire to be useful meeting the desire to be recognized. The work requires you to set aside both desires. The idea needs to land where it can be acted on. If that means someone else presents it, if that means you do not get credit, if that means the idea changes as it moves through other people, then that is what needs to happen. Your attachment to the idea as yours will limit where it can go. Your willingness to let it become theirs will expand where it can land.

**p57** — There is a difference between letting go of credit and letting go of responsibility. You can let go of credit and still hold responsibility for whether the work actually helps. Holding this responsibility requires you to stay engaged after the decision is made. It requires you to watch what happens when the idea meets the reality of daily work. It requires you to notice when the idea is being implemented in a way that will not work. It requires you to speak up when you see the implementation failing, even when speaking up feels like criticism of a decision that has already been made.

**p58** — The implementation will reveal what your proposal did not capture. The proposal existed in the abstract. The implementation exists in the specific. The specific always differs from the abstract in ways that matter. People will interpret your proposal through their own understanding of what needs to happen. They will adapt it to fit constraints you did not know existed. They will skip steps that seemed essential to you because those steps do not make sense in their context. None of this means the proposal was wrong. It means the proposal was incomplete, and the incompleteness only shows up in implementation.

**p59** — You will need to watch the implementation without taking it over. Watching without taking over requires you to tolerate seeing things done differently than you would do them. It requires you to distinguish between differences that matter and differences that do not. It requires you to speak up when something will cause harm and stay quiet when something is just different. This discernment comes from understanding the goal well enough to know what is essential and what is flexible.

**p60** — The goal will shift as the work progresses. The goal you started with was based on the information you had at the beginning. You have more information now. The goal needs to reflect what you have learned. This does not mean the original goal was wrong. It means the original goal was incomplete. Updating the goal is not failure. It is responsiveness to the reality you are working inside.

**p61** — Resistance will show up. It will show up in ways that are hard to name. Meetings that cannot be scheduled. Decisions that get deferred. People who were engaged who become quiet. People who were supportive who become critical. None of this will be framed as resistance. It will be framed as competing priorities, as timing issues, as needing more information. The framing is not dishonest. It is the language the system uses to protect itself from change that feels too fast, too risky, too destabilizing.

**p62** — You will be tempted to interpret resistance as a problem to be solved. The temptation comes from the belief that if you could just explain it better, if you could just get the right people in the room, if you could just find the right incentive, the resistance would go away. The resistance will not go away. The resistance is information about what the system is protecting. Understanding what is being protected gives you information about what it would take to move forward.

**p63** — Sometimes what is being protected is competence that will become obsolete. The person who has been carrying the weight has built strength around carrying it. Taking the weight away makes that strength unnecessary. The person may not want to admit this. They may not even be aware of it. They may frame their resistance in terms of risk, or timing, or feasibility. Underneath the framing is the fear of becoming less valuable, less needed, less competent in the new way of working.

**p64** — Sometimes what is being protected is a relationship that would be damaged by the change. The person has built trust with someone who would be harmed by what you are proposing. They cannot tell you this directly because it would require them to name a relationship that is not supposed to factor into the decision. They resist in ways that seem irrational from your perspective. From their perspective, they are protecting something that matters more than the outcome you are pursuing.

**p65** — Sometimes what is being protected is the stability of the system itself. The system has learned to survive by maintaining its shape. Your proposal would change the shape. The system responds through the people who inhabit it. The people may not be aware that they are protecting the system. They may experience their resistance as concern about risk, or about feasibility, or about timing. The concern is real. The system would be less stable during the transition. The question is whether the instability is tolerable and for how long.

**p66** — You will need to decide which resistance to work with and which to work around. Working with resistance requires you to understand what is being protected and find a way to honor the protection while still moving forward. Working around resistance requires you to find a path that does not require the resister's consent. Both approaches have their place. Working with resistance builds stronger foundations. Working around resistance moves faster. The choice depends on whether the work requires the resister's ongoing participation or whether it can succeed without them.

**p67** — You will make mistakes in reading the resistance. You will work with resistance that should have been worked around. You will work around resistance that should have been worked with. You will interpret protection as obstruction. You will interpret obstruction as protection. These mistakes will teach you something about the system and about your own tendencies. The mistakes are not failures if you learn from them. They become failures only if you repeat them without learning.

**p68** — The resistance will teach you something about your own relationship to resistance. You may feel frustration when people do not see what you see. You may feel impatience when people move slower than you think they should. You may feel anger when people protect things that seem unimportant to you. These feelings are information about your own expectations, your own timeline, your own attachment to the outcome. The feelings are not wrong. They are signals about what is being activated in you.

**p69** — You will need to find your own way of working with the feelings without letting them drive. Some people need to step back and process alone. Some people need to talk it through with someone who is not in the system. Some people need to write about what is happening to create distance between themselves and the situation. Some people need to name the feeling out loud to someone who can hear it without trying to fix it. The method matters less than the discipline of not acting from the feeling while it is at its peak.

**p70** — The resistance will shift as the work progresses. What was resistance at the beginning may become support later. What was support at the beginning may become resistance later. The shift does not mean people are being inconsistent. It means the work has moved into territory that activates different concerns. The person who supported you when the work was abstract may resist when the work becomes concrete. The person who resisted when the work was abstract may support when the work becomes concrete. Both shifts are information about what each person can carry and when.

**p71** — Power will shape everything that happens. Power is not just the formal authority described in the org chart. Power is the ability to make decisions, to allocate resources, to set priorities, to define what counts as success. Power is also the ability to block decisions, to withhold resources, to delay priorities, to redefine success. Power lives in people, not in positions. The person with the title may have less power than the person with the relationships, the history, the credibility, the information.

**p72** — You will need to map the power in the system. This mapping is not about creating a diagram. It is about learning who actually makes things happen and who actually stops things from happening. It is about learning who people listen to when they are uncertain. It is about learning who can absorb blame and who cannot. It is about learning who has the patience to stay with something until it lands and who needs things to happen quickly. This learning happens through observation, through conversation, through paying attention to what happens after the meeting ends.

**p73** — You will need to decide how to relate to the power you find. You may need to influence it. You may need to work around it. You may need to build enough power of your own to counterbalance it. You may need to find allies who already have power and align your work with their priorities. You may need to protect people who are vulnerable to the exercise of power. You may need to challenge power directly when it is causing harm. Each approach has its place. Each approach has its risks.

**p74** — Influencing power requires you to understand what the person with power cares about. This is not about manipulation. It is about finding the overlap between what you are trying to achieve and what they are already trying to achieve. The overlap may be small. It may be the only place where movement is possible. Working from the overlap does not mean abandoning your goals. It means finding a path that moves toward your goals while also moving toward theirs.

**p75** — Working around power requires you to understand where the power does not reach. There are always places where the person with power does not have visibility, does not have interest, does not have the relationships to enforce their will. These places are where you can experiment, build evidence, create momentum that the power cannot easily stop. The work done in these places may need to be reframed when it becomes visible. The reframing is not dishonesty. It is translation into language the power can hear.

**p76** — Building your own power requires you to understand what kind of power is available to you. You may have expertise that people need. You may have relationships that give you access to information. You may have the credibility that comes from having done this before. You may have the patience to stay with something longer than others will. You may have the willingness to have conversations that others are avoiding. None of this is formal authority. All of it is power that can be used carefully to move the work forward.

**p77** — Using power requires you to understand the cost of using it. Every time you use power, you spend some of the credibility, some of the relationships, some of the patience that others have for you. You can get it back, but it takes time. Using power too quickly leaves you without enough to finish the work. Using power too slowly leaves the work stuck. The discernment about when to use power and when to hold it comes from understanding how much you have and how much you will need.

**p78** — There is power in naming what is happening. Naming creates shared language. Shared language creates the possibility of shared action. The naming must be accurate enough that people recognize themselves in it. The naming must be generous enough that people do not feel attacked. The naming must be specific enough that it points to something that can be addressed. This is harder than it sounds. It requires you to find words that land as description rather than judgment.

**p79** — There is power in asking questions that reveal what people already know. The questions are not about extracting information. They are about creating the conditions where people can say what they have been unable to say. The right question at the right time can unlock a conversation that has been stuck. The wrong question can shut down a conversation that was beginning to open. The discernment about which questions to ask comes from listening to what people are ready to say.

**p80** — There is power in deciding what to carry and what to reflect back. You will hear things that you could repeat to create leverage. You will hear things that you could use to embarrass someone into action. You will hear things that you could weaponize if you were trying to win rather than trying to help. Carrying what you hear without weaponizing it builds trust. Reflecting back what you hear in a way that helps people hear themselves builds understanding. Both uses of power serve the work.

**p81** — Capability will move through the system. This movement is what people mean when they say they want to build capacity. The movement is slower and messier than the language suggests. Capability does not move through documentation. It does not move through training sessions. It does not move through the installation of tools. Capability moves through people doing things together, through the transfer of judgment that happens when someone watches someone else navigate a situation, through the conversations that happen after something goes wrong.

**p82** — You will be tempted to measure capability by the artifacts it produces. The documentation that gets written. The training that gets delivered. The tools that get adopted. These artifacts are not capability. They are evidence that activity has happened. Capability is what remains when the artifacts are no longer being used. Capability is the judgment that people have developed about how to work in this system, about what to pay attention to, about when to follow the process and when to adapt it.

**p83** — The capability that matters is the capability to handle situations that the process does not cover. The process covers the situations that have been handled before. The situations that have not been handled before will require judgment. The judgment comes from experience. The experience comes from being allowed to make decisions and seeing what happens. The allowing is the work. The allowing requires you to step back when your instinct is to step in. The allowing requires you to tolerate mistakes that could have been prevented if you had just done it yourself.

**p84** — You will need to decide which mistakes are tolerable and which are not. The mistakes that damage relationships in ways that cannot be repaired are not tolerable. The mistakes that cause harm to people who did not consent to the risk are not tolerable. The mistakes that violate commitments that were made to others are not tolerable. The mistakes that are part of learning to work in a new way are tolerable. The mistakes that reveal gaps in understanding are tolerable. The mistakes that create opportunities for conversation are tolerable. This discernment is the work of protecting people while still allowing them to learn.

**p85** — The capability will shift as the work progresses. The capability you started building was based on the work as you understood it at the beginning. The work has changed. The capability needs to change with it. This does not mean the original capability building was wrong. It means it was incomplete. Updating the capability is not failure. It is responsiveness to the reality you are working inside.

**p86** — You will need to decide when to leave. The leaving is part of the work. Staying too long creates dependency. Leaving too soon leaves the work unfinished. The decision about when to leave is not about you. It is about whether the system can continue without you. The system can continue without you when the people who need to carry the work have the capability to carry it. The system can continue without you when the relationships that support the work exist independently of you. The system can continue without you when the judgment that is needed lives in the people who will remain.

**p87** — The leaving will feel like loss. You have built relationships. You have invested energy. You have cared about the outcome. Letting go of something you have cared about is loss. The loss is real. Acknowledging the loss does not mean you are making the wrong decision. It means you are human and the work was human and the relationships were real.

**p88** — You will need to decide what you are leaving behind. You are leaving behind the work as you understood it. The work will continue without you. It will change in ways you cannot predict. The people who remain will adapt it to their needs. They will make mistakes you would not have made. They will find solutions you would not have found. This is not failure. This is the work becoming theirs.

**p89** — You will need to decide what you are taking with you. You are taking with you the learning about how systems work. You are taking with you the learning about your own responses, your own triggers, your own relationship to power. You are taking with you the learning about what you can carry and what you cannot. This learning will shape the next work you do. The learning is the capability you are building in yourself.

**p90** — The leaving is not an ending. The work continues. The system continues. The people continue. You continue. The connections you formed will persist in ways you may not see. The work you did will persist in ways you may not recognize. The learning you gained will persist in ways you will carry into the next system you enter. Nothing ends cleanly. Everything continues in a different form.

**p91** — There is a pattern to how systems learn. The learning does not happen through the formal mechanisms. It does not happen through the training programs, the documentation, the process updates. It happens through the conversations that happen after something goes wrong. It happens through the adaptations that people make when the process does not fit the situation. It happens through the stories that get told about what worked and what did not.

**p92** — The learning drifts over time. What was learned gets adapted. What was adapted gets forgotten. What was forgotten gets rediscovered. The drift is not failure. The drift is the system responding to changing conditions. The drift becomes a problem when the learning that is being lost is the learning that keeps people safe. The drift becomes a problem when the adaptations that are being forgotten were the adaptations that made the work possible.

**p93** — You will need to decide what learning to preserve and what learning to let drift. The learning that preserves the capability to handle novel situations is worth preserving. The learning that preserves the relationships that support the work is worth preserving. The learning that preserves the judgment about when to follow the process and when to adapt it is worth preserving. The learning that preserves specific solutions to specific problems can be allowed to drift. The specific problems will not come back in the same form. The capability to handle novel problems will be needed again.

**p94** — The preservation happens through the people who carry the learning. It does not happen through the artifacts. The artifacts can support the preservation. They cannot replace it. The person who knows why the process works the way it works is more valuable than the documentation that describes the process. The person who knows who to call when something goes wrong is more valuable than the escalation matrix. The person who knows what can be adapted and what cannot is more valuable than the rules about what can be adapted.

**p95** — You will need to decide who carries the learning. The learning should not live in one person. The learning that lives in one person is lost when that person leaves. The learning should live in multiple people who can reinforce it for each other. The learning should live in the relationships between people who can remind each other when the drift starts to take them somewhere they do not want to go. The learning should live in the conversations that happen regularly enough that the learning stays current.

**p96** — The learning will be challenged by new people entering the system. The new people will not have the history. They will not understand why things work the way they work. They will propose changes that the system has already tried and abandoned. This is not a failure of the new people. This is the natural state of systems where people come and go. The work of integrating new people includes the work of transmitting the learning without overwhelming them with history they cannot yet use.

**p97** — The learning will be challenged by changing conditions. What worked in one context may not work in another. What worked at one scale may not work at another. What worked with one set of people may not work with another. The learning needs to be adaptable enough to respond to changing conditions without losing the core insight that makes it valuable. This adaptability is the difference between learning that is alive and learning that is dead.

**p98** — You will need to decide when to let the learning go. The learning you have may not fit the new situation. The learning you have may be preventing you from seeing what is actually happening. The learning you have may be attached to a version of yourself that you are no longer becoming. Letting go of learning is harder than acquiring it. The learning became part of how you understand the work. Letting it go requires you to update your understanding of what the work is.

**p99** — The learning that matters is the learning about how you learn. The specific insights will become outdated. The situations will change. The capability to learn from what is happening, to adapt your understanding, to update your approach, this capability does not become outdated. This capability is what you are building when you pay attention to how you are learning, not just what you are learning.

**p100** — The systems we work inside are learning systems. They learn through the people who inhabit them. They learn through the adaptations that get made. They learn through the conversations that happen after something goes wrong. They learn through the stories that get told about what worked and what did not. The learning is not controlled. The learning is not designed. The learning happens. The work is to pay attention to what is being learned and decide whether it is the learning that will keep the system healthy.

**p101** — There is a difference between process that serves the work and process that serves itself. The process that serves the work makes the work easier to do. It reduces the cognitive load. It creates shared language. It creates predictable handoffs. It creates the conditions where people can do their best work. The process that serves itself exists to be followed. It creates work that does not need to be done. It creates meetings that do not need to happen. It creates documentation that will not be read. It creates the appearance of work without the substance.

**p102** — You will need to learn to distinguish between the two. The distinction is not always clear. The process that served the work at one point may have become process that serves itself. The process that serves itself may have been put in place for a reason that made sense at the time. The reason may no longer apply. The process may remain. The work of distinguishing requires you to ask what work the process is doing and whether that work needs to be done.

**p103** — The process that serves itself persists because it is easier to keep it than to remove it. Removing process requires someone to take responsibility for the removal. It requires someone to decide that the process is not needed. It requires someone to tolerate the discomfort of people who liked the process. It requires someone to accept the risk that something will be lost in the removal. Keeping the process requires nothing. The default is to keep. The work is to decide when to remove.

**p104** — You will encounter process that was put in place to solve a problem that no longer exists. The problem was real when the process was created. The problem has been solved, or has become irrelevant, or has been replaced by a different problem. The process remains. The process may now be causing problems of its own. The people who follow the process may not know why the process exists. They may not know that the problem it was solving is gone. They may experience the process as the way the work is done.

**p105** — You will encounter process that was put in place to protect against a risk that is no longer present. The risk was real when the process was created. The risk has been mitigated, or has been accepted, or has been replaced by a different risk. The process remains. The process may now be preventing the work from adapting to the current risks. The people who follow the process may not know what risk they are protecting against. They may experience the process as caution rather than as protection against a specific thing.

**p106** — You will encounter process that was put in place because someone with power wanted visibility into the work. The visibility served a purpose for the person with power. The visibility may not serve any purpose for the people doing the work. The process may create work that takes time away from the actual work. The people doing the actual work may experience the process as a tax on their time. They may comply without believing in the value of what they are doing. They may do the minimum required to avoid consequences.

**p107** — You will encounter process that was put in place to create the appearance of control. The control is not real. The process creates the appearance that something is being managed. The thing is not actually being managed. The process creates reports that show progress. The progress is not actually happening. The process creates meetings where status is reported. The status is not actually accurate. The process serves the need to feel in control without creating actual control.

**p108** — You will need to decide what to do with the process that serves itself. You can remove it. You can adapt it to serve the work. You can leave it in place and work around it. Each approach has its place. Removing it creates clarity and risks conflict. Adapting it creates relevance and risks dilution. Working around it creates momentum and risks duplication. The choice depends on whether the process can be changed, whether the change is worth the cost, whether the work can succeed with the process in place.

**p109** — The process that serves the work will need to evolve as the work evolves. The process that worked at one stage of the work may not work at another stage. The process that worked with one team may not work with another team. The process that worked in one context may not work in another context. The evolution requires someone to pay attention to whether the process is still serving the work. The attention is the work. The attention requires you to ask the question regularly and to be willing to change the answer.

**p110** — The honesty about process is the honesty about what work is being done and why. The honesty requires you to name when process is serving itself. The honesty requires you to name when process is creating the appearance of work without the substance. The honesty requires you to name when you are complying without believing. The honesty is risky. It challenges the legitimacy of things that people have invested in. It challenges the authority of people who benefit from the appearance of control. It challenges the comfort of people who prefer the certainty of process to the uncertainty of actual work. The honesty is necessary. Without it, the work becomes the performance of work. The performance is easier to measure. The performance is easier to manage. The performance is not the work.

**p111** — Modernization happens in place. The system does not stop while you modernize it. The work continues. The customers continue to expect the work to be done. The people continue to carry the weight while you are trying to change what the weight is made of. This constraint shapes everything about how the modernization can happen. You cannot rebuild the plane while it is on the ground. You must rebuild it while it is flying.

**p112** — The modernization will take longer than it should. This is not failure. This is the reality of changing a system that cannot stop. The work of modernization includes the work of keeping the system running. The work of keeping the system running takes time away from the work of modernization. The timeline that does not account for this is a timeline that exists in a world where the system can stop. The system cannot stop. The timeline needs to reflect the reality.

**p113** — The modernization will require you to run two systems in parallel. The old system does what it has always done. The new system is being built. The two systems need to coexist until the new system can take over. The coexistence creates work. The work of keeping the two systems in sync. The work of translating between the two systems. The work of deciding when to move something from the old system to the new. The work of moving it back when the new system does not work as expected.

**p114** — The parallel systems create confusion. People will not know which system to use. They will use the old system because it is familiar. They will use the new system because they want to help. They will use both systems and create work for themselves. The confusion is not a failure of communication. The confusion is the natural state of a system in transition. The work is to reduce the confusion without eliminating it entirely. Eliminating the confusion would require stopping the transition. The transition cannot stop. The confusion must be managed.

**p115** — The modernization will reveal dependencies you did not know existed. The old system has grown connections to other systems over time. The connections are not documented. The connections are not understood. The connections show up when the new system does not have them and something breaks. The breaking teaches you about the dependency. The learning comes at the cost of the break. The breaks will happen. The work is to make the breaks small enough that they can be absorbed and to learn from each break about what else might be connected.

**p116** — The modernization will require you to make decisions without complete information. The complete information does not exist. The complete information would require you to understand the entire system in ways that are not possible. The decisions must be made anyway. The decisions will be wrong in ways you cannot predict. The wrongness will show up later. The later will be too late to prevent. The work is to make the decisions reversible where possible and to monitor for the wrongness showing up.

**p117** — The modernization will change power dynamics in ways you cannot control. The old system had power distributed in a particular way. The new system will distribute power differently. The people who had power in the old system may lose power in the new system. The people who did not have power in the old system may gain power in the new system. The shifts will create resistance. The resistance will not be framed as resistance. It will be framed as concern about risk, or about feasibility, or about timing. The framing is not dishonest. It is the language the system uses to process the shift.

**p118** — The modernization will change what competence looks like. The competence that was valued in the old system may not be valued in the new system. The people who were competent in the old system may need to become competent in the new system. The becoming takes time. The time creates vulnerability. The vulnerability creates fear. The fear creates resistance. The resistance is not about the modernization. It is about the loss of competence and the uncertainty about whether the new competence can be built.

**p119** — The modernization will succeed or fail based on factors you cannot control. You can build the new system correctly. You can manage the transition carefully. You can communicate clearly. You can do everything right. The modernization can still fail because the organization changes priorities. Because the person with power leaves. Because the budget gets cut. Because the market shifts. Because something else becomes more important. The lack of control is not failure. It is the reality of working inside a system that is larger than the work you are doing.

**p120** — The modernization will teach you something about your own relationship to control. You may feel responsible for outcomes you cannot control. You may feel like you should have anticipated things you could not have anticipated. You may feel like the failure is yours even when the failure was caused by factors outside your influence. These feelings are not accurate. They are the result of caring about the work and wanting it to succeed. The feelings can be acknowledged without being believed. The work can be done well even when the outcome is not what you hoped for.

**p121** — Governance is the work of deciding who decides. The decisions about who decides are more important than the decisions themselves. The decisions about who decides shape what decisions can be made. The decisions about who decides shape who has power. The decisions about who decides shape who is responsible when things go wrong.

**p122** — Governance happens whether it is named or not. The absence of formal governance does not mean there is no governance. It means the governance is informal. It means the decisions about who decides are being made through relationships, through history, through power dynamics that are not named. The informal governance is harder to see. It is harder to change. It is harder to hold accountable.

**p123** — You will need to understand the governance that exists before you can change it. The governance that exists is the governance that is actually operating, not the governance that is described in the charter. The governance that is operating may be different from the governance that is described. The difference is information about how power actually works in this system.

**p124** — You will need to decide whether to work within the existing governance or to change it. Working within it is faster. It requires less political capital. It creates less resistance. It also limits what can be changed. Changing it is slower. It requires more political capital. It creates more resistance. It also enables changes that would not be possible otherwise. The choice depends on what you are trying to achieve and whether it can be achieved within the existing governance.

**p125** — The governance will shape what you can do. The governance will shape who you need to influence. The governance will shape what decisions require approval. The governance will shape who can allocate resources. The governance will shape who is accountable for outcomes. Understanding the governance allows you to work within it strategically. Ignoring the governance does not make it go away. It makes you vulnerable to it.

**p126** — You will need to decide what governance to leave behind. The governance you leave will shape what happens after you leave. The governance that concentrates power in one person will create a bottleneck. The governance that distributes power too widely will create paralysis. The governance that is not named will create confusion. The governance that is too rigid will create workarounds. The governance that is too flexible will create inconsistency. The balance is not a formula. It is a judgment about what this system needs.

**p127** — The governance will need to evolve as the system evolves. The governance that worked at one stage may not work at another stage. The governance that worked with one team may not work with another team. The governance that worked in one context may not work in another context. The evolution requires someone to pay attention to whether the governance is still serving the system. The attention is the work. The attention requires you to ask the question regularly and to be willing to change the answer.

**p128** — Ownership is the willingness to carry responsibility for outcomes. Ownership cannot be assigned. It can only be taken. The person who takes ownership is the person who feels responsible for the outcome even when they are not formally accountable. The person who does not take ownership is the person who does what they are told and lets the outcome be someone else's problem.

**p129** — You will need to decide where you want ownership to live. The ownership can live with you. The ownership can live with the people you are working with. The ownership can live with the person with formal accountability. The ownership can be distributed across multiple people. Each distribution has its consequences. Ownership that lives with you creates dependency. Ownership that lives with the people you are working with creates capability. Ownership that lives with the person with formal accountability creates clarity. Ownership that is distributed creates resilience and confusion.

**p130** — You will need to decide how to encourage ownership without creating burden. The encouragement happens through trust, through autonomy, through the willingness to let people make decisions and see what happens. The burden happens through accountability without authority, through responsibility without resources, through expectations without support. The difference between encouragement and burden is the difference between ownership that grows and ownership that shrinks.

Pushing against them creates growth and risks harm. Accepting them creates peace and risks stagnation. Working around them creates momentum and risks avoidance. Expanding them creates capability and requires time. The choice depends on what the limit is and what the situation requires.

**p137** — The work will teach you something about your own relationship to helping. You will discover why you help. You will discover what you get from helping. You will discover what helping costs you. You will discover when helping is actually helping and when it is something else. The learning is not comfortable. **p138** — You will need to decide what to do with the learning. You can ignore it. You can use it to judge yourself. You can use it to adjust how you help. You can use it to decide when not to help. Each approach has its place. Ignoring it allows you to continue without questioning. Judging yourself creates guilt and does not create change. Adjusting how you help creates more effective helping. Deciding when not to help creates boundaries that protect you and the people you would otherwise try to help.

**p139** — The work will become part of your story. You will tell it to yourself. You will tell it to others. The telling will shape how you understand what happened. The telling will shape what you learned from it. The telling will shape what you do next. The story is not the truth. It is your understanding of the truth. The understanding will change over time. **p140** — You will need to decide what story to tell. The story can be about success. The story can be about failure. The story can be about learning. The story can be about the system. The story can be about yourself. Each story emphasizes different things. Each story obscures different things. The choice of story shapes what you carry forward and what you leave behind. The choice is not about accuracy. It is about what you need the story to do for you.

**p142** — You will leave before the work is done. This is not failure. This is the reality of working inside systems that will outlast you. The work was never yours to complete. **p143** — The work will continue without you. This is what you were trying to achieve. The capability you built will be used. The relationships you formed will persist. **p144** — You will carry the work with you. The learning will shape what you do next. The relationships will persist in some form. The story will become part of how you understand yourself. It allows you to learn from them faster. It allows you to forgive yourself when they happen.

**p151** — There are practices that support the work. They are not techniques. They are not methods. They are ways of being in the work that make the work possible. The practices do not guarantee success. They do not prevent mistakes. They create the conditions where you can notice what is happening and respond with something other than reaction.

**p152** — The practice of arriving without answers. You enter a system with the intention of helping. You have experience. You have learning. You have ideas about what might work. The practice is to hold these lightly enough that they can be updated by what you learn here. The answers you brought were formed in other systems, with other people, under other conditions. They may apply. They may not. The practice is to listen first, to understand what is happening here, to let the answers emerge from this context rather than importing them from elsewhere.

**p153** — The practice of naming what you feel. Something happens in the room. You feel tension in your body. You feel frustration, or confusion, or sadness. The practice is to name the feeling without making it someone else's problem. You can say, "I am feeling frustrated right now." You do not say, "You are making me frustrated." The naming creates space between the feeling and the action. The space allows you to choose how to respond.

**p154** — The practice of asking questions you do not know the answer to. This is different from asking questions to extract information. This is asking questions because you genuinely do not know. The questions reveal your own uncertainty. They reveal that you are learning. They give other people permission to not know. The questions create the conditions where learning can happen together.

**p155** — The practice of noticing when you are defending. You propose something. Someone responds with a concern. You feel yourself tightening. You feel the urge to explain, to justify, to convince. The practice is to notice the defense without acting from it. The defense is information about your own attachment to the idea. The attachment may be valid. It may also be preventing you from hearing what the concern is actually saying.

**p156** — The practice of slowing down when things feel urgent. The urgency is real. The pain is real. The cost of inaction is real. The practice is to recognize that urgency often creates the conditions where mistakes happen. Slowing down does not mean stopping. It means moving at a pace that allows you to notice what is happening. It means moving at a pace that allows others to move with you.

**p157** — The practice of checking your understanding. You think you understand what someone is saying. You think you understand what is happening. The practice is to check. You can say, "Here is what I am hearing. Is that accurate?" You can say, "Here is what I think is happening. What am I missing?" The checking reveals gaps in your understanding before you act from them.

**p158** — The practice of naming power without accusation. You notice power dynamics at play. You notice who is being heard and who is not. You notice who can make decisions and who cannot. The practice is to name what you see without framing it as a moral failure. You can say, "I notice that the people closest to this work are not in the room." You do not say, "You are excluding the people who matter." The naming without accusation creates the possibility of change without triggering defense.

**p159** — The practice of deciding what to carry forward. You hear something in confidence. You hear something that could be useful. You hear something that could create leverage. The practice is to decide consciously what you will carry forward and what you will not. You can say, "I am going to carry this forward because it helps me understand the situation." You can say, "I am not going to carry this forward because it would damage trust." The deciding is the practice.

**p160** — The practice of ending conversations without resolution. Some conversations will not resolve. The tension will remain. The practice is to end the conversation without pretending it has been resolved. You can say, "We have not resolved this. I think we understand each other better. I think we should continue this conversation later." The ending without false resolution honors the complexity without collapsing it.

**p161** — There are conversations that matter. They are not the conversations that are scheduled. They are not the conversations that appear in the agenda. They are the conversations that happen in the margins, in the hallways, in the moments before and after the official meetings. These conversations are where the real work happens.

**p162** — The conversation before the meeting. Someone arrives early. You arrive early. You talk about something other than the agenda. The conversation reveals what is actually on their mind. The conversation reveals what they are worried about. The conversation reveals what they need from the meeting that is not on the agenda. The conversation shapes what happens in the meeting.

**p163** — The conversation after the meeting. The meeting ends. People linger. Someone says, "Can I talk to you for a minute?" The conversation reveals what they could not say in the room. The conversation reveals what they need you to understand. The conversation reveals what they are willing to commit to that they were not willing to commit to publicly.

**p164** — The conversation that happens when someone trusts you enough to tell you the truth. They have been waiting for the right moment. They have been waiting to see if you can be trusted. They have been watching how you respond to other people. They have been deciding whether to tell you what is actually happening. The conversation happens. The truth is shared. The trust is established. The work can begin.

**p165** — The conversation that happens when you need to tell someone something they do not want to hear. You have been waiting for the right moment. You have been deciding how to say it. You have been weighing the cost of telling them against the cost of not telling them. The conversation happens. The truth is shared. The relationship may be damaged. The relationship may be strengthened. The work requires the conversation.

**p166** — The conversation that happens when you need to ask for something you need. You have been carrying something alone. You have been trying to handle it yourself. You have been telling yourself you should not need help. The conversation happens. You ask for what you need. The asking is the work. The asking creates the possibility of receiving.

**p167** — The conversation that happens when you need to say no. You have been saying yes. You have been taking on more than you can carry. You have been telling yourself you should be able to handle it. The conversation happens. You say no. The no is the work. The no creates the boundary that allows you to continue.

**p168** — The conversation that happens when you need to end the relationship. The work is done. The work cannot continue. The relationship has served its purpose. The conversation happens. You name the ending. You acknowledge what was accomplished. You acknowledge what was not. You part ways. The ending is the work. The ending allows both of you to move on.

**p169** — The conversations that do not happen. You decide not to have the conversation. You decide the cost is too high. You decide the timing is wrong. You decide the relationship cannot sustain it. The decision not to have the conversation is also the work. The decision requires you to accept that some things will remain unsaid. The acceptance is the work.

**p170** — The conversations accumulate. They create the fabric of the work. They create the relationships that make the work possible. They create the understanding that allows the work to move forward. The conversations are not preparation for the work. The conversations are the work.

**p171** — There is a rhythm to the work. It is not the rhythm of the project plan. It is not the rhythm of the milestones. It is the rhythm of the work itself. The rhythm has phases. The phases are not linear. They cycle. They repeat. They spiral.

**p172** — The phase of arriving. You enter a system. You do not know what is happening. You are learning. You are listening. You are building understanding. The phase feels like confusion. It feels like not knowing. It feels like you should know more than you do. The phase is necessary. The understanding you are building is the foundation for everything that comes after.

**p173** — The phase of proposing. You have built enough understanding to propose something. The proposal reveals what you still do not understand. The proposal reveals the resistance. The proposal reveals the power dynamics. The phase feels like testing. It feels like learning through response. It feels like you are getting somewhere and also like you are stuck.

**p174** — The phase of working with resistance. You have encountered resistance. You are learning what the resistance is protecting. You are learning how to work with it or around it. The phase feels like struggle. It feels like you are pushing against something that does not want to move. It feels like you are making progress and also like you are not.

**p175** — The phase of momentum. Something shifts. The resistance eases. The power dynamics become clearer. The proposals start to land. The phase feels like flow. It feels like the work is moving. It feels like you can see the end. The phase is temporary. The momentum will shift. The work will require something different.

**p176** — The phase of implementation. The proposals are being implemented. The work is becoming concrete. The gaps between the proposal and the reality are showing up. The phase feels like adjustment. It feels like you are learning what you did not anticipate. It feels like you are fixing things and also like you are discovering new things to fix.

**p177** — The phase of capability transfer. The people you are working with are building the capability to continue without you. They are making decisions. They are solving problems. They are adapting the work to their needs. The phase feels like letting go. It feels like you are becoming less necessary. It feels like success and also like loss.

**p178** — The phase of leaving. You are preparing to leave. You are tying up loose ends. You are having the conversations that need to happen before you go. You are deciding what to carry forward and what to leave behind. The phase feels like ending. It feels like completion and also like incompleteness. It feels like you are done and also like the work will continue without you.

**p179** — The phase of integration. You have left. You are integrating the learning. You are deciding what the experience means. You are deciding what story to tell. You are deciding what to carry into the next work. The phase feels like processing. It feels like you are making sense of what happened. It feels like you are becoming someone who has done this work.

**p180** — The rhythm cycles. You will enter another system. You will arrive again. You will learn again. You will propose again. You will work with resistance again. You will find momentum again. You will implement again. You will transfer capability again. You will leave again. You will integrate again. The cycle is not failure. The cycle is the work. The work is cyclical. The work is not linear.
