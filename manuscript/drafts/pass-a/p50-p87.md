**p50** — You have built enough understanding to propose something. The proposal itself will teach you what you still do not understand. People will respond to your proposal in ways that reveal information you could not have accessed any other way. The proposal functions as a probe that reveals the shape of the system by how the system responds to it, and this revealing happens through the specific ways people engage with what you have put forward, through the questions they ask, through the concerns they raise, through the silences that appear when certain aspects of the proposal land in the room.

**p51** — The first proposal will not be the one that lands, and this outcome belongs to the work of learning where the actual obstacles live rather than the obstacles described in the charter or discussed in the early conversations. The obstacles show up when you propose something that would actually change how people work, when the abstract commitment to improvement meets the concrete reality of changing daily habits and established workflows. Someone will say they support the change and then not make the decision that would enable it. Someone will say the timing is wrong. Someone will say they need more information. Someone will say nothing at all. Each response teaches you something about where the weight actually lives in this system, about which decisions require which people, about what kind of justification moves things forward and what kind leaves them suspended.

**p52** — You will learn to read the responses. The ones who say yes and mean it. The ones who say yes and cannot deliver. The ones who say no and will help you find another way. The ones who say nothing and hold the power to block everything. The ones who say one thing in the room and another thing after the room empties. Each person navigates their own constraints, their own incentives, their own understanding of what is possible here, and understanding these constraints gives you information about what it would take to move them even though the understanding itself does not remove the constraints or make the movement easy.

**p53** — Moving someone requires you to understand what they are protecting. Sometimes they are protecting themselves from risk. Sometimes they are protecting someone else from consequences. Sometimes they are protecting the system from instability. Sometimes they are protecting you from a failure you do not yet see. The protection looks like resistance from where you are standing. From where they are standing, it looks like responsibility. Both perspectives hold accuracy, and the work becomes finding a path that honors the protection while still moving forward, which means acknowledging what would be lost or damaged by the change and finding ways to address that loss rather than dismissing it as an obstacle to be overcome.

**p54** — You will need to find the people who can actually make decisions. This is not always the people with the titles. Sometimes the person who can make the decision is the one who has been here long enough to know what will stick and what will not. Sometimes it is the person who carries the relationships that would be damaged if the decision went the wrong way. Sometimes it is the person who has the credibility to absorb the blame if things go wrong. Finding these people requires you to watch how decisions actually get made, not how they are supposed to get made, and this watching happens in the conversations after the meeting, in the phone calls that happen before the formal review, in the informal check-ins that determine whether something has enough support to proceed.

**p55** — Once you find the decision-makers, you will need to understand what they need to feel safe making the decision. Safety lives in having enough cover, enough support, enough evidence that the decision will not cost them more than they can afford. The evidence they need takes different forms. Sometimes it is the visible support of other people they trust. Sometimes it is a pilot small enough that failure would not be noticed. Sometimes it is simply time, the time to let the idea circulate until it feels like it came from them, until the idea has moved through enough conversations that adopting it feels like recognizing something that was already emerging rather than making a risky bet on something new.

**p56** — You will be tempted to claim credit for ideas that need to come from someone else. This temptation holds both the desire to be useful and the desire to be recognized, and the work requires setting aside both desires so the idea can land where it can be acted on. If that means someone else presents it, if that means you do not get credit, if that means the idea changes as it moves through other people, then that is what needs to happen. Your attachment to the idea as yours will limit where it can go. Your willingness to let it become theirs will expand where it can land, because the idea needs to take root in the soil of their ownership rather than remaining something you brought in from outside.

**p57** — There is a difference between letting go of credit and letting go of responsibility. You can let go of credit and still hold responsibility for whether the work actually helps. Holding this responsibility requires you to stay engaged after the decision is made. It requires you to watch what happens when the idea meets the reality of daily work. It requires you to notice when the idea is being implemented in a way that will not work. It requires you to speak up when you see the implementation failing, even when speaking up feels like criticism of a decision that has already been made, even when it would be easier to let the failure happen and say you told them so.

**p58** — The implementation will reveal what your proposal did not capture. The proposal existed in the abstract. The implementation exists in the specific. The specific always differs from the abstract in ways that matter. People will interpret your proposal through their own understanding of what needs to happen. They will adapt it to fit constraints you did not know existed. They will skip steps that seemed essential to you because those steps do not make sense in their context. The proposal was incomplete, and this incompleteness only shows up in implementation when the abstract meets the concrete and the gaps between them become visible through the work of people trying to make it real.

**p59** — You will need to watch the implementation without taking it over. Watching without taking over requires you to tolerate seeing things done differently than you would do them. It requires you to distinguish between differences that matter and differences that do not. It requires you to speak up when something will cause harm and stay quiet when something is just different. This discernment comes from understanding the goal well enough to know what is essential and what is flexible, from holding the purpose of the work clearly enough that you can recognize when the purpose is being served even through methods that differ from your own.

**p60** — The goal will shift as the work progresses. The goal you started with was based on the information you had at the beginning. You have more information now. The goal needs to reflect what you have learned. The original goal was incomplete, and updating it reflects responsiveness to the reality you are working inside, a willingness to let the goal evolve as your understanding of the system deepens through the actual work of trying to change it.

**p61** — Resistance will show up. It will show up in ways that are hard to name. Meetings that cannot be scheduled. Decisions that get deferred. People who were engaged who become quiet. People who were supportive who become critical. The system frames this through competing priorities, through timing issues, through needing more information, and this framing belongs to the language the system uses to protect itself from change that feels too fast, too risky, too destabilizing.

**p62** — You will be tempted to interpret resistance as a problem to be solved. The temptation comes from the belief that if you could just explain it better, if you could just get the right people in the room, if you could just find the right incentive, the resistance would go away. The resistance carries information about what the system is protecting, and understanding what is being protected gives you information about what it would take to move forward, about what needs to be addressed before the movement can happen.

**p63** — Sometimes what is being protected is competence that will become obsolete. The person who has been carrying the weight has built strength around carrying it. Taking the weight away makes that strength unnecessary. The person may not want to admit this. They may not even be aware of it. They may frame their resistance in terms of risk, or timing, or feasibility. Underneath the framing lives the fear of becoming less valuable, less needed, less competent in the new way of working, and this fear operates whether or not it is named aloud.

**p64** — Sometimes what is being protected is a relationship that would be damaged by the change. The person has built trust with someone who would be harmed by what you are proposing. They cannot tell you this directly because it would require them to name a relationship that is not supposed to factor into the decision. They resist in ways that seem irrational from your perspective. From their perspective, they are protecting something that matters more than the outcome you are pursuing, and this protection makes sense within the web of relationships they are navigating even when it appears as obstruction from where you stand.

**p65** — Sometimes what is being protected is the stability of the system itself. The system has learned to survive by maintaining its shape. Your proposal would change the shape. The system responds through the people who inhabit it. The people may not be aware that they are protecting the system. They may experience their resistance as concern about risk, or about feasibility, or about timing. The concern is real. The system would be less stable during the transition. The question becomes whether the instability is tolerable and for how long, and this question requires judgment about what the system can absorb without breaking.

**p66** — You will need to decide which resistance to work with and which to work around. Working with resistance requires you to understand what is being protected and find a way to honor the protection while still moving forward. Working around resistance requires you to find a path that does not require the resister's consent. Both approaches have their place. Working with resistance builds stronger foundations. Working around resistance moves faster. The choice depends on whether the work requires the resister's ongoing participation or whether it can succeed without them, and this assessment itself requires reading the system accurately enough to know which path will lead somewhere the work can take root.

**p67** — You will make mistakes in reading the resistance. You will work with resistance that should have been worked around. You will work around resistance that should have been worked with. You will interpret protection as obstruction. You will interpret obstruction as protection. These mistakes will teach you something about the system and about your own tendencies. The mistakes become part of your learning when you reflect on them and adjust your approach, when you notice the patterns in your own misreadings and begin to correct for them.

**p68** — The resistance will teach you something about your own relationship to resistance. You may feel frustration when people do not see what you see. You may feel impatience when people move slower than you think they should. You may feel anger when people protect things that seem unimportant to you. These feelings carry information about your own expectations, your own timeline, your own attachment to the outcome, and noticing this information gives you a choice about how to respond to it.

**p69** — You will need to find your own way of working with the feelings without letting them drive. Some people need to step back and process alone. Some people need to talk it through with someone who is not in the system. Some people need to write about what is happening to create distance between themselves and the situation. Some people need to name the feeling out loud to someone who can hear it without trying to fix it. The method matters less than the discipline of not acting from the feeling while it is at its peak, of creating enough space between the feeling and the response that the response can be chosen rather than reactive.

**p70** — The resistance will shift as the work progresses. What was resistance at the beginning may become support later. What was support at the beginning may become resistance later. The shift reflects the work moving into territory that activates different concerns. The person who supported you when the work was abstract may resist when the work becomes concrete. The person who resisted when the work was abstract may support when the work becomes concrete. Both shifts carry information about what each person can carry and when, about where their own boundaries and capacities lie in relation to the change you are proposing.

**p71** — Power will shape everything that happens. Power lives beyond the formal authority described in the org chart. Power is the ability to make decisions, to allocate resources, to set priorities, to define what counts as success. Power is also the ability to block decisions, to withhold resources, to delay priorities, to redefine success. Power lives in people, not in positions. The person with the title may have less power than the person with the relationships, the history, the credibility, the information.

**p72** — You will need to map the power in the system. This mapping happens through learning who actually makes things happen and who actually stops things from happening. It is about learning who people listen to when they are uncertain. It is about learning who can absorb blame and who cannot. It is about learning who has the patience to stay with something until it lands and who needs things to happen quickly. This learning happens through observation, through conversation, through paying attention to what happens after the meeting ends, through noticing which conversations actually determine the direction of things.

**p73** — You will need to decide how to relate to the power you find. You may need to influence it. You may need to work around it. You may need to build enough power of your own to counterbalance it. You may need to find allies who already have power and align your work with their priorities. You may need to protect people who are vulnerable to the exercise of power. You may need to challenge power directly when it is causing harm. Each approach has its place. Each approach carries its risks, and choosing among them requires reading the situation accurately enough to know which approach will serve the work without causing damage that outweighs the potential benefit.

**p74** — Influencing power requires you to understand what the person with power cares about. This work involves finding the overlap between what you are trying to achieve and what they are already trying to achieve. The overlap may be small. It may be the only place where movement is possible. Working from the overlap does not mean abandoning your goals. It means finding a path that moves toward your goals while also moving toward theirs, which requires understanding their priorities well enough to see where alignment exists and where it does not.

**p75** — Working around power requires you to understand where the power does not reach. There are always places where the person with power does not have visibility, does not have interest, does not have the relationships to enforce their will. These places are where you can experiment, build evidence, create momentum that the power cannot easily stop. The work done in these places may need to be reframed when it becomes visible. The reframing becomes translation into language the power can hear, a way of presenting what has already happened in terms that make it difficult to undo.

**p76** — Building your own power requires you to understand what kind of power is available to you. You may have expertise that people need. You may have relationships that give you access to information. You may have the credibility that comes from having done this before. You may have the patience to stay with something longer than others will. You may have the willingness to have conversations that others are avoiding. All of this is power that can be used carefully to move the work forward, power that operates through influence and relationships rather than formal authority.

**p77** — Using power requires you to understand the cost of using it. Every time you use power, you spend some of the credibility, some of the relationships, some of the patience that others have for you. You can get it back, but it takes time. Using power too quickly leaves you without enough to finish the work. Using power too slowly leaves the work stuck. The discernment about when to use power and when to hold it comes from understanding how much you have and how much you will need, from reading the situation well enough to know when spending power will move things forward and when it will leave you depleted before the work is done.

**p78** — There is power in naming what is happening. Naming creates shared language. Shared language creates the possibility of shared action. The naming must be accurate enough that people recognize themselves in it. The naming must be generous enough that people do not feel attacked. The naming must be specific enough that it points to something that can be addressed. This is harder than it sounds. It requires you to find words that land as description rather than judgment, words that help people see what is happening without feeling defensive about their role in it.

**p79** — There is power in asking questions that reveal what people already know. The questions create the conditions where people can say what they have been unable to say. The right question at the right time can unlock a conversation that has been stuck. The wrong question can shut down a conversation that was beginning to open. The discernment about which questions to ask comes from listening to what people are ready to say, from sensing what is close to the surface and what needs more time before it can be spoken.

**p80** — There is power in deciding what to carry and what to reflect back. You will hear things that you could repeat to create leverage. You will hear things that you could use to embarrass someone into action. You will hear things that you could weaponize if you were trying to win rather than trying to help. Carrying what you hear without weaponizing it builds trust. Reflecting back what you hear in a way that helps people hear themselves builds understanding. Both uses of power serve the work, and choosing between them requires judgment about what will move the work forward without damaging the relationships that make the work possible.

**p81** — Capability will move through the system. This movement is what people mean when they say they want to build capacity. The movement is slower and messier than the language suggests. Capability moves through people doing things together, through the transfer of judgment that happens when someone watches someone else navigate a situation, through the conversations that happen after something goes wrong, through the gradual accumulation of experience that cannot be accelerated through documentation or training sessions or the installation of tools.

**p82** — You will be tempted to measure capability by the artifacts it produces. The documentation that gets written. The training that gets delivered. The tools that get adopted. These artifacts hold evidence that activity has happened. Capability lives in what remains when the artifacts are no longer being used. Capability is the judgment that people have developed about how to work in this system, about what to pay attention to, about when to follow the process and when to adapt it, and this judgment shows up in the decisions people make when no one is watching and when the process does not provide clear guidance.

**p83** — The capability that matters is the capability to handle situations that the process does not cover. The process covers the situations that have been handled before. The situations that have not been handled before will require judgment. The judgment comes from experience. The experience comes from being allowed to make decisions and seeing what happens. The allowing is the work. The allowing requires you to step back when your instinct is to step in. The allowing requires you to tolerate mistakes that could have been prevented if you had just done it yourself, and this tolerance requires trusting that the learning from the mistake will be more valuable than the efficiency of doing it yourself.

**p84** — You will need to decide which mistakes are tolerable and which are not. The mistakes that damage relationships in ways that cannot be repaired are not tolerable. The mistakes that cause harm to people who did not consent to the risk are not tolerable. The mistakes that violate commitments that were made to others are not tolerable. The mistakes that are part of learning to work in a new way are tolerable. The mistakes that reveal gaps in understanding are tolerable. The mistakes that create opportunities for conversation are tolerable. This discernment is the work of protecting people while still allowing them to learn, and it requires judgment about what kind of mistake will strengthen the system and what kind will weaken it.

**p85** — The capability will shift as the work progresses. The capability you started building was based on the work as you understood it at the beginning. The work has changed. The capability needs to change with it. The original capability building was incomplete, and updating it reflects responsiveness to the reality you are working inside, a willingness to let the capability evolve as the work evolves and as your understanding of what the system needs deepens through the actual experience of working within it.

**p86** — You will need to decide when to leave. The leaving is part of the work. Staying too long creates dependency. Leaving too soon leaves the work unfinished. The decision about when to leave belongs to the question of whether the system can continue without you. The system can continue without you when the people who need to carry the work have the capability to carry it. The system can continue without you when the relationships that support the work exist independently of you. The system can continue without you when the judgment that is needed lives in the people who will remain, and recognizing this moment requires honesty about whether your presence is still necessary or whether it has become a crutch that prevents the system from standing on its own.

**p87** — The leaving will feel like loss. You have built relationships. You have invested energy. You have cared about the outcome. Letting go of something you have cared about is loss. The loss is real. Acknowledging the loss honors the humanity of the work and the relationships and the care you have invested, and this acknowledgment sits alongside the recognition that leaving is the right decision even though it carries the weight of loss.

**p88** — You will need to decide what you are leaving behind. You are leaving behind the work as you understood it. The work will continue without you. It will change in ways you cannot predict. The people who remain will adapt it to their needs. They will make mistakes you would not have made. They will find solutions you would not have found. The work becomes theirs through this adaptation, through this ownership that requires them to make their own relationship with the work rather than inheriting yours.
