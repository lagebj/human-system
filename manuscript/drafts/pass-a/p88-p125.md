**p88** — You will need to decide what you are leaving behind. You are leaving behind the work as you understood it. The work will continue without you because the system has its own momentum. It will change in ways you cannot predict because the people who remain will bring their own history and judgments. They will make mistakes you would not have made, and those mistakes will teach them things you could not have taught because the learning comes from living through the consequence. They will find solutions you would not have found because they stand in a different position with different constraints. The work becomes theirs, which means it will land differently in the system than you envisioned, and you have to release your attachment to the particular shape it takes.

**p89** — You will need to decide what you are taking with you. You are taking with you the learning about how systems work, not as abstract principles but as felt knowledge about what it feels like when a system is ready to change. You are taking with you the learning about your own responses, your own triggers, your own relationship to power. You are taking with you the learning about what you can carry and what you cannot, the weight that sits in your chest when you have held too much tension. This learning will shape the next work you do because you will enter the next system with different eyes. The learning is the capability you are building in yourself, the kind that shows up in how you listen, how you wait, how you recognize resistance that protects something real.

**p90** — The leaving is not an ending because the work continues in the people who remain, the system continues with its own rhythms, and you continue walking forward. The connections you formed will persist in ways you may not see because relationships transform rather than end. The work you did will persist in ways you may not recognize because interventions will be adapted or abandoned or become so integrated people forget they were interventions. The learning you gained will persist as a sensibility, a way of paying attention you cannot turn off. Nothing ends cleanly because human systems do not operate in clean boundaries, they bleed into each other and leave traces. Everything continues in a different form.

**p91** — There is a pattern to how systems learn. The learning does not happen through the formal mechanisms because they are designed for compliance and documentation. It does not happen through the training programs scheduled quarterly, or the documentation written once and never updated. It happens through the conversations that happen after something goes wrong, the ones in hallways and Slack channels where people try to make sense without a script. It happens through the adaptations people make when the process does not fit the situation. It happens through the stories that get told about what worked and what did not, retold until they become part of the system's understanding.

**p92** — The learning drifts as conditions change and people come and go. What was learned gets adapted because the situation no longer exists in the same form. What was adapted gets forgotten because people leave or the work becomes routine. What was forgotten gets rediscovered when the same problem surfaces. Drift is what happens when learning is alive rather than frozen, when it responds to changing conditions. The drift becomes a problem when the learning being lost is what keeps people safe. The drift becomes a problem when the adaptations being forgotten were what made the work possible through collective ingenuity never documented.

**p93** — You will need to decide what learning to preserve and what learning to let drift. The learning that preserves the capability to handle novel situations is worth preserving because novel situations will keep appearing. The learning that preserves the relationships that support the work is worth preserving because damaged relationships cannot carry difficult work. The learning that preserves judgment about when to follow process and when to adapt is worth preserving. The learning that preserves specific solutions to specific problems can be allowed to drift because those problems will not return in the same form. The capability to handle novel problems will be needed again.

**p94** — The preservation happens through the people who carry the learning because people can hold the reasoning, not just the learning itself. It does not happen through the artifacts because artifacts capture what was decided but not why. The artifacts can support preservation by providing reference points. They cannot replace it because an artifact cannot answer unanticipated questions or adapt when conditions change. The person who knows why the process works is more valuable than the documentation. The person who knows who to call is more valuable than the escalation matrix because the matrix does not capture relationships. The person who knows what can be adapted is more valuable than the rules.

**p95** — You will need to decide who carries the learning. The learning should not live in one person because it is lost when that person leaves. The learning should live in multiple people who can reinforce it for each other. The learning should live in the relationships between people because relationships create accountability based on mutual care. The learning should live in the conversations that happen regularly enough that it stays current, conversations where someone asks why and someone else explains.

**p96** — The learning will be challenged by new people entering the system. The new people will not have the history because they were not there when problems were discovered. They will not understand why things work because the why is embedded in stories that have become invisible. They will propose changes the system has already tried because they do not know what was tried. The new people cannot know what they were not taught, and the teaching is the responsibility of the system. This is the natural state of systems where people come and go. The work of integrating new people includes transmitting the learning without overwhelming them.

**p97** — The learning will be challenged by changing conditions. What worked in one context may not work in another because context includes people, tools, constraints. What worked at one scale may not work at another because scaling introduces new failure modes. What worked with one set of people may not work with another. The learning needs to be adaptable enough to respond without losing the core insight. This adaptability is the difference between learning that is alive and learning that is dead.

**p98** — You will need to decide when to let the learning go. The learning you have may not fit the new situation. The learning you have may be preventing you from seeing what is actually happening because you are looking for patterns you recognize. The learning you have may be attached to a version of yourself you are no longer becoming. Letting go of learning is harder than acquiring it because the learning became part of your identity. Letting it go requires you to update your understanding of what the work is.

**p99** — The learning that matters is the learning about how you learn because specific insights will become outdated. The situations will change. The capability to learn from what is happening, to adapt your understanding, to update your approach, this capability does not become outdated. This capability is what you are building when you pay attention to how you are learning, when you notice when you are applying old solutions without checking the fit. The capability shows up in how quickly you recognize when something is not working.

**p100** — The systems we work inside are learning systems. They learn through the people who inhabit them because people notice patterns. They learn through the adaptations that get made. They learn through the conversations after something goes wrong. They learn through the stories that get told. The learning is not controlled because it is distributed and emergent. The learning is not designed because it arises from people doing work together. The learning happens whether you pay attention or not, and the work is to pay attention to what is being learned and decide whether it will keep the system healthy.

**p101** — There is a difference between process that serves the work and process that serves itself. The process that serves the work makes the work easier by reducing cognitive load, creating shared language, creating predictable handoffs. The process that serves itself exists to be followed, and it creates work that does not need to be done, meetings that do not need to happen, documentation that will not be read, the appearance of work without the substance.

**p102** — You will need to learn to distinguish between the two. The distinction is not always clear because a process that served the work may have become process that serves itself. The process that serves itself may have been put in place for a reason that made sense at the time. The reason may no longer apply. The process may remain because processes have inertia. The work of distinguishing requires you to ask what work the process is doing and whether that work needs to be done.

**p103** — The process that serves itself persists because it is easier to keep than to remove. Removing process requires someone to take responsibility, to decide the process is not needed, to tolerate discomfort, to accept risk. Keeping the process requires nothing. The work is to decide when to remove, weighing the cost of keeping against the cost of removing.

**p104** — You will encounter process that was put in place to solve a problem that no longer exists. The problem was real when the process was created. The problem has been solved or has become irrelevant. The process remains because no one has revisited whether it is still needed. The process may now be causing problems. The people who follow may not know why it exists. They may experience the process as the way work is done, invisible as a choice.

**p105** — You will encounter process that was put in place to protect against a risk that is no longer present. The risk was real when the process was created. The risk has been mitigated or accepted. The process remains. The process may now be preventing adaptation. The people who follow may not know what risk they are protecting against. They may experience the process as caution, and caution feels virtuous even when purposeless.

**p106** — You will encounter process that was put in place because someone with power wanted visibility. The visibility served the person with power. The visibility may not serve the people doing the work. The process may create work that takes time from the actual work. The people may experience the process as a tax. They may comply without believing. They may do the minimum to avoid consequences.

**p107** — You will encounter process that was put in place to create the appearance of control. The control is not real. The process creates the appearance of management through dashboards and reports. The thing is not actually being managed. The process creates reports that show progress, but the progress is completing steps rather than achieving outcomes. The process creates meetings where status is reported, but the status is optimized for acceptability. The process serves the need to feel in control.

**p108** — You will need to decide what to do with the process that serves itself. You can remove it, which creates clarity but risks conflict. You can adapt it, which preserves investment but risks dilution. You can work around it, which avoids conflict but risks duplication. The choice depends on whether the process can be changed, whether the change is worth the cost. Removing creates clarity and risks conflict. Adapting creates relevance and risks dilution. Working around creates momentum and risks duplication.

**p109** — The process that serves the work will need to evolve. The process that worked at one stage may not work at another. The process that worked with one team may not work with another. The evolution requires someone to pay attention to whether the process is still serving the work. The attention is the work, an ongoing practice of noticing and adjusting.

**p110** — The honesty about process is the honesty about what work is being done and why. The honesty requires you to name when process is serving itself. The honesty requires you to name when process is creating appearance without substance. The honesty requires you to name when you are complying without believing. The honesty is risky because it challenges legitimacy and authority and comfort. The honesty is necessary because without it, the work becomes performance.

**p111** — Modernization happens in place. The system does not stop while you modernize it because customers and the business depend on it. The work continues. The customers continue to expect the work at the same quality. The people continue to carry the weight while you change it. This constraint shapes everything. You cannot rebuild the plane while it is on the ground. You must rebuild it while flying, which means every change must be reversible, every step tested in production.

**p112** — The modernization will take longer than it should. The timeline that does not account for the constraint exists in a world where systems can be paused, and this system cannot. This is the reality of changing a system that cannot stop. The work of modernization includes keeping the system running. The work of keeping running takes time from modernization. The timeline needs to reflect that modernization in place is slower, that the constraint is structural.

**p113** — The modernization will require you to run two systems in parallel. The old system does what it has always done. The new system is being built. The two systems need to coexist. The coexistence creates work because there are two systems to maintain. The work of keeping them in sync. The work of translating between them. The work of deciding when to move something. The work of moving it back when the new system does not work.

**p114** — The parallel systems create confusion. People will not know which system to use. They will use the old system because it is familiar. They will use the new system because they want to help. They will use both and create work. Confusion is inherent in running two systems. The confusion is the natural state of transition. The work is to reduce the confusion without eliminating it because eliminating it would require stopping. The transition cannot stop. The confusion must be managed.

**p115** — The modernization will reveal dependencies you did not know existed. The old system has grown connections through years of operation. The connections are not documented. The connections are not understood. The connections show up when the new system does not have them and something breaks. The breaking teaches you. The learning comes at the cost of the break. The breaks will happen. The work is to make the breaks small enough to be absorbed, to learn from each break.

**p116** — The modernization will require you to make decisions without complete information. The complete information does not exist. The decisions must be made anyway. The decisions will be wrong in ways you cannot predict. The wrongness will show up later. The later will be too late to prevent. The work is to make the decisions reversible where possible, to monitor for wrongness, to be willing to change course.

**p117** — The modernization will change power dynamics. The old system had power distributed one way. The new system will distribute power differently. The people who had power may lose power. The people who did not have power may gain power. The shifts will create resistance. The resistance will be framed as concern about risk or feasibility or timing, and people may genuinely believe the concerns because loss of power feels like risk. It is the language the system uses to process the shift.

**p118** — The modernization will change what competence looks like. The competence valued in the old system may not be valued in the new. The people who were competent may need to become competent in the new system. The becoming takes time. The time creates vulnerability. The vulnerability creates fear. The fear creates resistance. The resistance is about the threat to competence and identity.

**p119** — The modernization will succeed or fail based on factors you cannot control. You can build correctly, manage carefully, communicate clearly. The modernization can still fail because the organization changes priorities, because the sponsor leaves, because the budget is cut, because the market shifts. The lack of control is a feature of working inside a larger system. It is the reality of working with competing priorities. The reality must be accepted.

**p120** — The modernization will teach you about your relationship to control. You may feel responsible for outcomes you cannot control. You may feel you should have anticipated the unanticipatable. You may feel the failure is yours. These feelings arise from caring and having a professional identity tied to outcomes. The feelings assume control you did not have. The feelings can be acknowledged without being believed. The work can be done well even when the outcome is not what you hoped.

**p121** — Governance is the work of deciding who decides. The decisions about who decides are more important than the decisions themselves. The decisions about who decides shape what decisions can be made, who has authority, who is accountable. The decisions about who decides shape who has power. The decisions about who decides shape who is responsible when things go wrong.

**p122** — Governance happens whether it is named or not. The absence of formal governance does not mean there is no governance. It means the governance is informal, operating through relationships, through history, through unnamed power dynamics. The informal governance is harder to see. It is harder to change. It is harder to hold accountable.

**p123** — You will need to understand the governance that exists before you can change it. The governance that exists is what actually operates, not what is described in the charter. The governance that is operating may differ from what is described because formal structure and practice have drifted. The difference is information about how power actually works.

**p124** — You will need to decide whether to work within the existing governance or to change it. Working within it is faster, requires less political capital, creates less resistance, but limits what can be changed. Changing it is slower, requires more political capital, creates more resistance, but enables changes otherwise impossible. The choice depends on what you are trying to achieve.

**p125** — The governance will shape what you can do. The governance will shape who you need to influence. The governance will shape what decisions require approval. The governance will shape who can allocate resources. The governance will shape who is accountable. Understanding the governance allows you to work within it strategically. Ignoring the governance does not make it go away. It makes you vulnerable.
