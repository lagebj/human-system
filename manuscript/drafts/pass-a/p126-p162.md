**p126** — You will need to decide what governance to leave behind. The governance you leave will shape what happens after you leave, extending your influence into a future you will not inhabit. The governance that concentrates power in one person will create a bottleneck where decisions accumulate and wait for a single point of approval. The governance that distributes power too widely will create paralysis where everyone has authority and no one has clarity about who moves forward. The governance that is not named will create confusion where people operate from different assumptions about who decides what. The governance that is too rigid will create workarounds where people find unofficial paths around official structures. The governance that is too flexible will create inconsistency where similar situations receive different treatment depending on who is involved. Finding the balance requires judgment about what this system needs at this stage of its development, and that judgment comes from paying attention to how decisions actually flow through the system rather than how they are supposed to flow.

**p127** — The governance will need to evolve as the system evolves, because the conditions that shaped the original governance will shift as the work progresses and the people change. The governance that worked at one stage may not work at another stage as the team grows or shrinks, as the stakes rise or fall, as the external pressures intensify or ease. The governance that worked with one team may not work with another team because different groups carry different histories, different levels of trust, different capacities for self-direction. The governance that worked in one context may not work in another context because the surrounding systems exert different pressures and create different constraints. The evolution requires someone to pay attention to whether the governance is still serving the system, and that attention means regularly asking whether the current arrangements are enabling the work or constraining it, and being willing to change the answer when the evidence suggests the governance has become misaligned with what the system needs.

**p128** — Ownership is the willingness to carry responsibility for outcomes, which means feeling the weight of what happens even when you lack formal authority to direct it. Ownership cannot be assigned because it lives in the internal relationship a person has with the work rather than in the external description of their role. It can only be taken when someone decides that the outcome matters to them personally and that they will carry concern for it regardless of whether anyone asks them to. The person who takes ownership feels responsible for the outcome even when they are not formally accountable, and that feeling drives them to notice gaps, to raise concerns, to offer help without waiting for permission. The person who does not take ownership does what they are told and lets the outcome be someone else's problem, which creates a system where work gets done but no one feels the whole.

**p129** — You will need to decide where you want ownership to live, and that decision shapes the entire dynamic of how the work proceeds. The ownership can live with you, which creates a pattern where people look to you for direction and wait for your judgment before moving forward. The ownership can live with the people you are working with, which creates capability as they develop the muscle of making decisions and carrying consequences. The ownership can live with the person with formal accountability, which creates clarity about who ultimately answers for what happens. The ownership can be distributed across multiple people, which creates resilience through shared responsibility and also creates confusion when it becomes unclear who moves when consensus does not emerge. Each distribution carries consequences that ripple through the system, and the choice depends on what you are trying to build and whether dependency or capability serves that aim better.

**p130** — You will need to decide how to encourage ownership without creating burden, because the same conditions that enable ownership can tip into conditions that overwhelm people with responsibility they cannot carry. The encouragement happens through trust, through autonomy, through the willingness to let people make decisions and see what happens without intervening to prevent mistakes they need to make to learn. The burden happens through accountability without authority, where people are held responsible for outcomes they cannot influence, through responsibility without resources, where people are expected to deliver without the means to deliver, through expectations without support, where people are told what success looks like without anyone asking what they need to reach it. The difference between encouragement and burden is the difference between ownership that grows because people feel trusted and capable, and ownership that shrinks because people feel set up to fail.

**p131** — The work changes you as you move through it, entering a system with the intention of changing it and discovering that the system changes you in the process through countless small adjustments you make without noticing. The change happens in ways that are not always visible to you or to others, accumulating through the small ways you adapt to the reality you are working inside as you learn what can be said and what cannot, what can be attempted and what will be blocked. The change happens in the judgments you make about what is possible, which shift as you encounter resistance and discover that some things you thought were fixed can move while other things you thought were flexible are actually rigid. The change happens in the relationships you form and the relationships you lose, as some people draw closer through shared work and others distance themselves when your presence complicates their position. The change happens in the learning that becomes part of how you understand the work, settling into your bones as experience that shapes how you approach the next system you enter.

**p132** — You will leave different than you arrived, shaped by the time you spent inside a system that affected you even as you tried to affect it. The systems you work inside are not external to you while you are in them, because you become part of the system the moment you enter it and the system begins to shape your thinking, your pacing, your sense of what is possible. The system affects you the way you affect the system, operating through the same channels of influence, the same relationships, the same power dynamics that you are trying to navigate and shift. The effect is not always what you intended or wanted, because the system has its own logic and its own momentum that operates independently of your intentions. What happens when you enter a system with the intention of changing it is that you become part of what changes, and the person who leaves is not the person who arrived.

**p133** — The work will activate things in you that you did not know were there, bringing to the surface reactions and responses that remained dormant until the specific conditions of this system triggered them. You will feel frustration you did not expect, rising up when you encounter resistance you thought you were prepared for but that feels different when you are inside it. You will feel anger you did not anticipate, emerging when you witness decisions that seem to ignore what you know to be true about the situation. You will feel helplessness you did not prepare for, settling in when you realize that some dynamics cannot be changed no matter how clearly you see them or how hard you work. You will feel satisfaction you did not plan for, arriving unexpectedly when something small works out in a way that suggests the work is moving forward. You will feel attachment you did not intend, developing toward people or outcomes that you thought you could hold more lightly. These activations are information about your own relationship to the work, revealing what you care about and where you are vulnerable and how you are affected by working inside systems that are larger than you and that will continue after you leave.

**p134** — You will need to decide what to do with the activations when they arise, because ignoring them does not make them go away and expressing them without discernment can create problems for yourself and others. You can ignore them and continue without processing, which allows you to keep moving but leaves the activations to accumulate and potentially surface later in less controlled ways. You can suppress them and continue without burdening others, which creates a kind of discipline but also creates distance between you and your own experience of the work. You can express them and release them, which creates relief for you and risks burdening others who may not have the capacity to hold what you are carrying. You can learn from them, which requires you to pay attention to what is being activated and why, to ask what this reaction reveals about your own relationship to the work and to the people you are working with and to the outcomes you are trying to create. Each approach has its place depending on the situation and the relationship and the timing, and the decision about which to use is itself part of the work.

**p135** — The work will teach you something about your own limits, revealing edges you did not know existed until you encountered situations that pressed against them. You will discover things you cannot carry, capacities that are smaller than you thought they were or burdens that are heavier than you anticipated. You will discover situations you cannot navigate, dynamics that you cannot untangle no matter how much you understand them or how skillfully you try to work with them. You will discover dynamics you cannot change, patterns that are too deeply embedded or too well protected or too central to the system's functioning to be shifted by your presence. You will discover expectations you cannot meet, demands that exceed what you can deliver or that require you to become someone you are not. The limits are information about what you can do and what you cannot do, and that information is useful for deciding what work to take on and what work to decline, for understanding where you can contribute and where you need to acknowledge that contribution is not possible.

**p136** — You will need to decide how to relate to your limits when you encounter them, because the limits themselves are fixed but your relationship to them can vary. You can push against them and create growth, which expands your capacity and risks harm when you push too hard or too fast or in ways that damage you or others. You can accept them and create peace, which allows you to work within your actual capacity and risks stagnation if acceptance becomes an excuse for not stretching at all. You can work around them and create momentum, which allows you to continue making progress and risks avoidance if working around becomes a pattern of never engaging with what is difficult. You can expand them and create capability, which builds long-term capacity and requires time that may not be available in the current situation. The choice depends on what the limit is and what the situation requires and what you can sustain without compromising your ability to continue doing this work.

**p137** — The work will teach you something about your own relationship to helping, revealing motivations and patterns that operate beneath your conscious awareness of why you do this work. You will discover why you help, tracing the impulse back through your own history and your own needs and your own understanding of what it means to be useful. You will discover what you get from helping, which may include satisfaction or validation or a sense of purpose or a way of managing your own anxiety about not doing enough. You will discover what helping costs you, in energy and time and emotional bandwidth and opportunities you decline because you are already committed to work that does not serve you. You will discover when helping is actually helping and when it is something else, when it is creating dependency rather than capability, when it is serving your need to be needed rather than serving the actual needs of the people you are trying to help. The learning is uncomfortable because it requires you to look at your own motivations without flinching and to acknowledge when helping serves you more than it serves the person you are helping, which does not mean you stop helping but that you help with more awareness of what you are doing and why.

**p138** — You will need to decide what to do with the learning when it emerges, because the learning itself does not change anything until you decide how to use it. You can ignore it and continue without questioning, which allows you to keep helping in the ways you have always helped without examining whether those ways are effective or sustainable. You can use it to judge yourself, which creates guilt and does not create change because judgment locks you into shame rather than opening a path toward different behavior. You can use it to adjust how you help, which creates more effective helping as you learn to offer what is actually needed rather than what you prefer to give or what makes you feel most useful. You can use it to decide when not to help, which creates boundaries that protect you and the people you would otherwise try to help, because helping without boundaries often creates patterns where you become depleted and the people you help become dependent. Each approach has its place depending on what you are learning and what the situation requires and what you can sustain.

**p139** — The work will become part of your story as you integrate it into your understanding of who you are and what you have done and what it meant. You will tell it to yourself, constructing a narrative that makes sense of the experience and that helps you carry it forward. You will tell it to others, sharing what happened in ways that are shaped by who is listening and why you are telling them and what you hope they will understand. The telling will shape how you understand what happened, because the story you construct emphasizes certain elements and minimizes others, and over time the story becomes your memory of what occurred. The telling will shape what you learned from it, because the learning is not fixed at the moment the work ends but continues to evolve as you tell the story differently in different contexts. The telling will shape what you do next, because the story you tell about this work influences what work you choose to take on and how you approach it. The story is your understanding of the truth rather than the truth itself, and that understanding will change over time as you integrate the experience into who you are becoming, which is not dishonesty but the natural process of making meaning from experience.

**p140** — You will need to decide what story to tell, because the choice of story shapes what you carry forward and what you leave behind. The story can be about success, emphasizing what was accomplished and what worked and what you contributed to the positive outcomes. The story can be about failure, emphasizing what did not work and what you could have done differently and what you learned from the gaps between intention and outcome. The story can be about learning, emphasizing how you changed and what you understand now that you did not understand before. The story can be about the system, emphasizing how the system functioned and what constraints it operated under and why it responded the way it did. The story can be about yourself, emphasizing who you were when you entered the work and who you became as you moved through it. Each story emphasizes different things and obscures different things, highlighting certain elements while leaving others in shadow. The choice is about what you need the story to do for you, whether you need it to validate your efforts or to hold you accountable or to help you understand what happened or to prepare you for the next work.

**p141** — There is no clean ending to this work because the work does not end when you leave but transitions into something else that continues without your direct involvement. It becomes someone else's work, taken up by people who remain and who will shape it according to their own understanding and their own priorities. It becomes a different kind of work, shifting from the work of building capability to the work of sustaining capability, from the work of creating change to the work of maintaining change. It becomes part of the history of the system, woven into the stories people tell about how things came to be the way they are. The history will be told in ways that may not match your understanding of what happened, because the telling serves different purposes for different people and because memory itself is unreliable and shaped by present needs. The telling is not yours to control once you leave, and the telling becomes part of how the system understands itself, influencing how people think about what is possible and what is not.

**p142** — You will leave before the work is done, because the work of changing human systems extends beyond the tenure of any individual and the systems themselves will outlast you. The work was never yours to complete but was yours to contribute to, adding your effort and your attention and your care to a larger effort that began before you arrived and will continue after you leave. The contribution is what matters, because it is the only part that was actually yours to give. The contribution is what remains, settling into the system as changed practices or shifted relationships or new capabilities that would not exist without your presence. The contribution is what shapes what happens next, influencing the trajectory of the work even when you are no longer there to direct it.

**p143** — The work will continue without you, which is what you were trying to achieve when you focused on building capability rather than dependency. The capability you built will be used by people who remain, applied to problems you encountered and problems you never saw. The relationships you formed will persist in some form, continuing to function even without your facilitation or your presence. The learning you shared will be applied, sometimes in ways you would recognize and sometimes in ways that would surprise you. The use and the persistence and the application will not be exactly what you envisioned, because the people who remain will adapt the work to their own needs and their own contexts and their own understanding of what matters. The adaptation is the work becoming theirs, which is the goal you were working toward even when it felt like loss of control.

**p144** — You will carry the work with you as you move forward, integrating it into who you are and how you approach the next system you enter. The learning will shape what you do next, informing your judgments and your pacing and your sense of what is possible. The relationships will persist in some form, continuing as professional connections or personal friendships or occasional check-ins that remind you of the work you did together. The story will become part of how you understand yourself, contributing to your sense of who you are as a practitioner and what you are capable of. The carrying is integration rather than burden, because you are not holding onto something that should be released but are allowing the experience to become part of your foundation. The integration is how the work continues in you even after you have left the system, shaping your approach to future work in ways that are often unconscious but always present.

**p145** — The next system will be different from this one, operating under different conditions and different constraints and different power dynamics. The learning from this system will not apply directly but will need to be adapted to the new context, which requires you to understand what was universal and what was specific to the system you just left. The adaptation is the work of learning a new system while carrying the learning from the old one, holding both in mind as you figure out what fits and what does not. The carrying can help by giving you patterns to recognize and questions to ask and mistakes to avoid. It can also hinder by creating assumptions that do not fit the new context or by creating expectations that the new system will respond the way the old system did. The learning that was useful in one context may not be useful in another, and the discernment about what to carry and what to leave is the work of staying flexible while staying grounded in what you have learned.

**p146** — You will make the same mistakes in the next system, because you are the same person carrying the same patterns and the same blind spots and the same ways of responding under pressure. The mistakes will be familiar, feeling like repetitions of patterns you have lived through before. They will feel different because the context is different, because the people are different and the stakes are different and the specific circumstances are different. They will be the same because you are the same, because the mistakes emerge from who you are and how you operate and what you notice and what you miss. The awareness of the mistakes does not prevent them from happening, because awareness operates at a different level than the automatic responses that create the mistakes. The awareness allows you to notice them sooner, to recognize the pattern as it is unfolding rather than only in retrospect. The awareness allows you to learn from them faster, to extract the learning without needing to repeat the mistake as many times. The awareness allows you to forgive yourself when they happen, because you understand that the mistakes are part of being human and doing work that matters.

**p147** — You will also make new mistakes as you grow and try new things and adapt to new contexts. The new mistakes are the result of trying new things, of experimenting with approaches you have not used before and taking risks you have not taken before. The new mistakes are the result of adapting to new contexts, of misreading situations that seemed familiar but were actually different in ways you did not initially perceive. The new mistakes are the result of being willing to be wrong in service of learning, of accepting that getting it wrong is part of getting it right over time. The willingness is what allows you to grow, because growth requires stepping into uncertainty and accepting that you will not always know what you are doing. The growth is what makes the work worth doing, because the work changes you in ways that matter to you and that prepare you for the next work.

**p148** — The work is worth doing even when it does not succeed in the ways you initially intended, because the success you can measure is not the only thing that matters. The work of paying attention is the point, because attention creates the possibility of seeing what is actually happening rather than what you think is happening. The work of caring is the point, because care creates the conditions where people feel seen and where the work can matter to them. The work of staying present with the tension is the point, because the tension contains information about what is actually at stake and what actually needs to change. The work of being human inside human systems is the point, because the systems are made of people and the change you are trying to create is change in how people work together. The work is its own justification, requiring no external validation to be worthwhile. The outcome is secondary because it is not entirely under your control, shaped by factors you cannot influence and decisions you cannot make. The work is under your control, the quality of attention and care and presence you bring to it, and that is enough.

**p149** — The systems we work inside are us, made of our decisions and our relationships and our work and our learning and our power and our governance and our ownership and our care. The systems are not external things that can be fixed from the outside, because there is no outside position from which to fix them. The systems are us working together, coordinating our efforts and negotiating our differences and building our capacity to do work that matters. The change we are trying to create is change in how we work together, in how we make decisions and how we share power and how we hold ourselves and each other accountable. The change starts with us, with the way we show up and the way we listen and the way we respond when things are difficult. It continues with us, requiring sustained attention and sustained effort and sustained willingness to stay present with the tension. It ends with us, because there is no one else to do this work and no other place for the change to come from.

**p150** — This is where the walk pauses, not as an ending but as a breath, a moment to acknowledge what has been said and what remains to be lived. The walk continues in the work you do, in the systems you enter, in the people you work with, in the learning you carry, in the care you bring. The walk continues as you move from one system to another, carrying what you have learned and leaving what you have contributed. You are the walk and the walk is you, because there is no separation between the person doing the work and the work being done. There is only the work and the people and the systems and the care and the learning and the power and the governance and the ownership, and the truth that none of it is separate from any of it. The walk continues.

**p151** — There are practices that support the work, ways of being in the work that make the work possible without guaranteeing any particular outcome. They are ways of being rather than techniques or methods, because techniques and methods can be applied mechanically while practices require you to show up with attention and intention. The practices do not guarantee success and do not prevent mistakes, because the work is too complex and the systems are too unpredictable for any practice to ensure a particular result. The practices create the conditions where you can notice what is happening and respond with something other than reaction, creating space between what occurs and how you respond to it.

**p152** — The practice of arriving without answers means entering a system with the intention of helping while holding your experience and your learning and your ideas about what might work lightly enough that they can be updated by what you learn here. The answers you brought were formed in other systems, with other people, under other conditions that may or may not match the conditions you are encountering now. They may apply in this context, or they may not, and the practice is to listen first and to understand what is happening here before deciding what to do. The practice is to let the answers emerge from this context rather than importing them from elsewhere, which requires patience and humility and a willingness to not know at the beginning what you will know at the end.

**p153** — The practice of naming what you feel means noticing when something happens in the room and you feel tension in your body, when you feel frustration or confusion or sadness rising up in response to what is occurring. The practice is to name the feeling without making it someone else's problem, saying something like "I am feeling frustrated right now" rather than "You are making me frustrated." The naming creates space between the feeling and the action, allowing you to feel the frustration without immediately acting from it. The space allows you to choose how to respond, to decide whether to express the frustration or set it aside or explore what is underneath it, rather than being driven by the feeling itself.

**p154** — The practice of asking questions you do not know the answer to is different from asking questions to extract information from other people. This is asking questions because you genuinely do not know, because there is something you need to understand and you are acknowledging that you do not already have the answer. The questions reveal your own uncertainty, showing that you are learning and that you do not have all the answers already formed. They reveal that you are learning alongside other people rather than teaching them what you already know. They give other people permission to not know, creating conditions where learning can happen together rather than flowing in one direction from expert to novice. The questions create the conditions where learning can happen together, where people can think out loud and explore possibilities without needing to have the answers ready.

**p155** — The practice of noticing when you are defending means paying attention to what happens in you when you propose something and someone responds with a concern. You feel yourself tightening, feeling the urge to explain or justify or convince the person that your idea is sound. The practice is to notice the defense without acting from it, to recognize that the tightening is happening without immediately moving to defend the idea. The defense is information about your own attachment to the idea, revealing that you have invested something in it beyond its practical merit. The attachment may be valid, because the idea may actually be good and worth pursuing. It may also be preventing you from hearing what the concern is actually saying, because the defense creates a barrier between you and the feedback that could help you improve the idea or recognize its limitations.

**p156** — The practice of slowing down when things feel urgent means recognizing that the urgency is real and the pain is real and the cost of inaction is real, while also recognizing that urgency often creates the conditions where mistakes happen. The practice is to move at a pace that allows you to notice what is happening, to pay attention to the dynamics at play and the decisions being made and the effects those decisions are having. Slowing down does not mean stopping, because sometimes action is required and delay would cause harm. It means moving at a pace that allows others to move with you, because work that requires coordination cannot happen faster than the people involved can move. It means moving at a pace that allows you to notice what is happening, because mistakes made in urgency often require more time to fix than the time that was saved by moving quickly.

**p157** — The practice of checking your understanding means recognizing that you think you understand what someone is saying and you think you understand what is happening, while also recognizing that your understanding may be incomplete or inaccurate. The practice is to check by saying something like "Here is what I am hearing. Is that accurate?" or "Here is what I think is happening. What am I missing?" The checking reveals gaps in your understanding before you act from them, allowing you to correct your understanding before you make decisions based on it. The checking also signals to other people that you are trying to understand them accurately, which builds trust and creates conditions where they are more likely to share information openly.

**p158** — The practice of naming power without accusation means noticing power dynamics at play and noticing who is being heard and who is not and noticing who can make decisions and who cannot. The practice is to name what you see without framing it as a moral failure, saying something like "I notice that the people closest to this work are not in the room" rather than "You are excluding the people who matter." The naming without accusation creates the possibility of change without triggering defense, because the observation is offered as information rather than as indictment. People can respond to information without needing to defend themselves, which creates space for them to consider whether the observation is accurate and whether something should be done about it.

**p159** — The practice of deciding what to carry forward means recognizing that you will hear things in confidence and hear things that could be useful and hear things that could create leverage, and that you need to decide consciously what you will carry forward and what you will not. You can say to yourself, "I am going to carry this forward because it helps me understand the situation," recognizing that some information is necessary for you to do the work effectively. You can say, "I am not going to carry this forward because it would damage trust," recognizing that some information should not be moved from one context to another even if it could be useful. The deciding is the practice, because the decision requires you to weigh the potential benefit against the potential cost and to make a conscious choice rather than carrying everything forward by default.

**p160** — The practice of ending conversations without resolution means recognizing that some conversations will not resolve and that the tension will remain even after the conversation ends. The practice is to end the conversation without pretending it has been resolved, saying something like "We have not resolved this. I think we understand each other better. I think we should continue this conversation later." The ending without false resolution honors the complexity without collapsing it, acknowledging that some things cannot be settled in one conversation and that pretending otherwise would create a false sense of progress. The practice allows you to end the conversation without forcing agreement, creating space for the tension to continue existing while the work continues around it.

**p161** — There are conversations that matter beyond the conversations that are scheduled and the conversations that appear in the agenda. They are the conversations that happen in the margins and in the hallways and in the moments before and after the official meetings, when people are less guarded and more willing to say what they actually think. These conversations are where the real work happens, because they are where people share what they cannot share in the formal setting and where they test ideas before bringing them into the room and where they build the relationships that make the formal work possible.

**p162** — The conversation before the meeting happens when someone arrives early and you arrive early and you talk about something other than the agenda. The conversation reveals what is actually on their mind, which may or may not be related to what is scheduled to be discussed. The conversation reveals what they are worried about, which may be the thing that will dominate their attention during the meeting even if it is not on the agenda. The conversation reveals what they need from the meeting that is not on the agenda, which may be reassurance or clarity or support or simply to be heard. The conversation shapes what happens in the meeting, because people carry into the room what they carried into the conversation, and the meeting proceeds differently depending on what was said before it officially began.
