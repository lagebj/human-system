# Inbox Note: Behaviour Changes Through the System Around It

## Purpose

Use these ideas when writing about change, enablement, ownership, capability, culture, leadership, and operating systems.

The central insight is simple:

People do not change merely because an organisation describes a better future, announces new values, provides training, or asks them to adopt a different mindset. Behaviour changes when the conditions surrounding real work make different actions possible, meaningful, and sustainable.

This supports the wider view that people are part of the system. Their behaviour cannot be separated from the structures, signals, constraints, relationships, and consequences around them.

## Start With What People Actually Do

Organisations often describe desired change through broad words:

* ownership
* collaboration
* trust
* agility
* engagement
* accountability
* confidence
* proactivity

These words may express a useful direction, but they do not describe observable actions.

Writing should distinguish between:

* an aspiration
* a state
* an outcome
* an actual behaviour

“Ownership” is not itself a behaviour. Ownership becomes visible when someone makes a decision, explains a trade-off, follows a change into production, handles a failure, contacts another team directly, or updates a working practice without waiting for outside instruction.

“Confidence” becomes visible when a team acts within understood boundaries, makes decisions with incomplete information, asks for review rather than direction, or recovers without handing responsibility back to someone else.

Abstract language is useful for orientation. Concrete actions are required to understand whether anything has changed.

## Outcomes Arrive Too Late to Manage Directly

Results are consequences of many actions interacting over time.

A successful release, improved customer experience, faster delivery, reduced cost, or increased independence is an outcome. None of these describes what people did to produce it.

Managing only through outcomes means looking at the system after the work has already happened.

The more useful question is:

What repeated actions are likely to contribute to the outcome, and what in the environment currently supports or prevents those actions?

This does not mean reducing complex work to simple metrics. It means tracing the path between intent and result.

## Behaviour Exists in Context

An action does not appear in isolation. It is influenced by what happens before it and what happens afterward.

Before the action there may be:

* instructions
* expectations
* access
* tools
* time
* examples from others
* decision rights
* reminders
* policies
* social signals
* fear of consequences

After the action there may be:

* trust
* praise
* blame
* delay
* extra work
* removal of authority
* support
* rescue by someone more senior
* silence
* visible success
* punishment for taking a reasonable risk

These surrounding conditions teach people how the organisation really works.

A company may say that teams own their systems while requiring approval for every meaningful decision. It may ask for experimentation while punishing failed attempts. It may request collaboration while measuring individual output. It may ask people to take initiative while senior staff repeatedly take over when uncertainty appears.

The written rule is only one signal. The lived consequence is often stronger.

## The Environment Is Usually the Stronger Lever

Change efforts often focus on changing people internally:

* improve the mindset
* create engagement
* build awareness
* communicate the vision
* change attitudes
* introduce new values

These approaches may have a role, but they are weak when the work environment continues to reward the old behaviour.

A person does not need a new mindset to use a simpler deployment path. A team does not need a culture programme to receive clearer decision rights. A developer does not need motivational training when the real blocker is missing access, an overloaded approval flow, or a platform nobody can understand.

When behaviour is difficult, inspect the system before judging the person.

This avoids turning structural failure into a personal deficiency.

## Culture Is a Pattern, Not a Separate Machine

Culture is often treated as an invisible force that causes behaviour.

A more practical view is that culture is a name given to patterns people repeatedly experience:

* who is allowed to decide
* what happens when someone disagrees
* whether bad news travels safely
* whether leaders keep their commitments
* whether help creates learning or dependence
* whether reasonable failure produces learning or blame
* whether stated ownership is supported by actual authority

From this perspective, culture is not changed through a separate culture initiative. It changes as the repeated actions, conditions, and consequences of work change.

This does not make culture unimportant. It makes culture less mystical.

To understand the culture, observe what people repeatedly do, what the system permits, and what it rewards.

## Behaviour Can Change Before Belief Does

Organisations often wait for complete agreement, motivation, confidence, or emotional commitment before changing how work happens.

This reverses the relationship.

People may develop confidence after successfully doing the work. They may begin to believe in a new approach after experiencing that it works. A team may become more willing to take ownership after it has been given a safe opportunity to exercise ownership.

Action can produce learning. Learning can change belief.

This supports enablement through real work:

* let the team make the decision
* let them deploy the change
* let them explain the trade-off
* let them handle a controlled failure
* remain close enough to prevent serious harm
* avoid taking the work back merely because uncertainty appears

Confidence is often the residue of survived experience.

## Capability Requires More Than Knowledge

Knowing what to do does not mean someone can do it.

For an action to occur, several conditions usually need to exist:

### Capability

Does the person or team have the knowledge, skill, understanding, and practical experience?

### Opportunity

Do they have time, access, tools, authority, support, and a workable environment?

### Motivation

Does acting make sense from their position? Do they expect the action to matter? Is it safer or more rewarding to continue with the old behaviour?

These conditions interact.

A highly motivated team may still be unable to act because it lacks access or authority. A capable team may stop trying because each initiative is overridden. A person may appear unmotivated after repeatedly learning that effort changes nothing.

The visible behaviour is real, but the explanation may live elsewhere in the system.

## Do Not Over-Psychologise the Problem

Understanding every deep cause of a person’s behaviour is neither possible nor appropriate.

The goal is not to diagnose people, manipulate their inner states, or explain their personalities. The useful focus is on actionable conditions around the work.

At the same time, this should not become an excuse to ignore structural causes.

There is an important distinction:

* Psychological speculation is usually unnecessary.
* Structural analysis is often essential.

A team’s behaviour may be shaped by ownership boundaries, incentives, contracts, hierarchy, access controls, budget, platform constraints, or leadership intervention. These are not personal traits. They are features of the operating system.

Change the closest actionable condition, but remain alert to the wider structure that may reproduce the same problem elsewhere.

## Power Must Be Included in the Model

Behavioural models can become too clean. Organisations are not neutral environments.

Some people can approve, block, fund, redefine, reward, punish, or ignore. Others carry responsibility without authority. Some teams are told to own outcomes while remaining dependent on groups with different incentives.

Any analysis of behaviour at work must therefore include:

* formal authority
* informal influence
* decision rights
* veto points
* access boundaries
* status
* career incentives
* budget ownership
* leadership behaviour
* the personal cost of acting

A team cannot behave its way out of a structure that prevents action.

Sometimes the intervention is not coaching the team. It is changing who is allowed to decide.

## Reinforcement Is Often Built Into the Work

Organisations teach behaviour through consequences, whether deliberately or not.

When someone takes initiative and a leader immediately takes over, dependence is reinforced.

When a team solves a problem and is then given greater trust, ownership is reinforced.

When every failure produces another approval gate, avoidance is reinforced.

When external experts repeatedly rescue the team, waiting is reinforced.

When a team receives credit, retains authority, and is trusted with the next decision, capability is reinforced.

The strongest reinforcement is not always praise or reward. It may be the continued right to act.

## Punishment Produces Side Effects

Pressure, blame, public criticism, and threat can suppress unwanted behaviour in the moment. They also teach people to hide information, avoid risk, shift responsibility, protect themselves, and minimise exposure.

The unwanted behaviour may disappear from view without the underlying problem being resolved.

This matters when writing about safety, trust, leadership, and accountability.

A system based on punishment may produce surface compliance while weakening the behaviours it needs most:

* honesty
* experimentation
* early escalation
* initiative
* shared responsibility
* learning from failure

Fear can create movement. It rarely creates durable capability.

## Behaviour Change Is Not Always More

Change may mean:

* starting a new action
* stopping an existing action
* doing something more often
* doing it less often
* acting earlier
* acting later
* reducing unnecessary intensity
* sustaining an action after support is removed

More is not automatically better.

More collaboration can become meeting overload. More review can become gatekeeping. More customer contact can become interruption. More standardisation can remove judgment. More help can create dependence.

Every useful practice has a point beyond which it becomes friction.

Writing should avoid treating desirable behaviour as unlimited.

## Change Has a Speed and a Half-Life

The speed of change matters.

Moving too slowly can preserve harmful conditions. Moving too quickly can create imitation without understanding, compliance without ownership, or dependence on the people driving the intervention.

Change must also be examined after it first appears.

A behaviour may:

* stabilise
* disappear
* reverse
* continue beyond its useful level
* survive only while external support remains
* evolve into a locally owned practice

Initial movement is not proof of durable change.

The test is what happens after the intervention becomes quieter.

## Withdrawal Reveals What Was Load-Bearing

External support can create a false picture of capability.

A team may appear independent while an experienced consultant is:

* framing every decision
* noticing every risk
* maintaining stakeholder relationships
* preparing every difficult conversation
* correcting work before failure becomes visible
* carrying the mental model for the whole system

The real test begins when that support recedes.

If the behaviour disappears, the team has not failed. The system has revealed which scaffold was still carrying weight.

Withdrawal is therefore not only an exit activity. It is a diagnostic method.

The movement from doing, to pairing, to reviewing, to advising, to leaving makes hidden dependence visible.

## The People Doing the Work Must Participate

Behavioural precision must not become behavioural control.

The organisation should not define a list of desired actions from a distance and then treat employees as targets to be adjusted.

The people doing the work understand constraints, trade-offs, and consequences that are invisible from above. They must participate in:

* defining the problem
* identifying the relevant actions
* describing the blockers
* interpreting current behaviour
* designing changes to the environment
* deciding how progress will be recognised
* evaluating whether the intervention helps

The difference is between designing people and designing with people.

This distinction should remain central to the manuscript.

## Measurement Is a Tool, Not the System

Observable behaviour can make vague change more concrete. Measurement can also become surveillance, theatre, and reduction.

Knowledge work cannot be fully represented through counts.

Use measurement only when it helps answer a real question:

* Is the team acting without external prompting?
* Are decisions moving closer to the work?
* Are blockers being resolved directly?
* Does ownership persist when support recedes?
* Are people using the new path in practice?
* Is the new behaviour producing the intended effect?

Measurement should be sparse, temporary, and used for learning.

When a measure stops helping people understand the system, it has expired.

## Do Not Accept the Stated Objective Uncritically

Many behaviour-change approaches begin with an organisational objective and work backward.

This assumes the objective is valid.

The stated goal may be a proxy, a symptom, or a politically acceptable version of the real problem.

“Deliver faster” may conceal unsafe release mechanisms.

“Improve collaboration” may conceal fragmented ownership.

“Build a platform” may conceal repeated coordination failure.

“Create accountability” may conceal unclear authority.

“Change the culture” may conceal leadership behaviour nobody wants to confront.

Before asking what people should do differently, examine whether the organisation is solving the right problem.

Optimising behaviour around a false objective makes the system more efficient at producing the wrong result.

## Relevance to the Book

These ideas should strengthen the manuscript’s existing view of systems:

* People are not outside the system.
* Behaviour is shaped by the environment around real work.
* Abstract ambitions need concrete expression.
* Capability develops through participation.
* Confidence often follows action.
* Help can reinforce dependence.
* Authority and responsibility must be examined together.
* Culture is visible in repeated behaviour and consequences.
* Leadership is expressed through the conditions leaders create.
* Durable change is revealed when scaffolding is removed.
* The stated problem should always remain open to challenge.

Do not turn this material into a standalone framework chapter unless the narrative requires it.

Use it as underlying reasoning in familiar situations:

* a leader asks people to take ownership but continues making every decision
* a transformation programme teaches new methods while leaving incentives untouched
* a team appears passive after being repeatedly overruled
* consultants create progress that stops when they leave
* a process introduced for safety survives after the original risk has disappeared
* an organisation announces trust while adding more controls
* people are blamed for behaviour that the system quietly rewards
* culture is discussed because specific leadership actions are harder to confront

The writing should remain human and situational. The theory belongs underneath the story, not on top of it.

## Core Mental Model

When behaviour in an organisation appears confusing, weak, resistant, or inconsistent:

1. Clarify the outcome being pursued.
2. Challenge whether that outcome addresses the real problem.
3. Describe the actions that would meaningfully contribute to it.
4. Observe what currently happens in the real work.
5. Examine what comes before the action.
6. Examine what follows the action.
7. Include authority, incentives, access, and power.
8. Change the nearest actionable condition.
9. Let people practise through meaningful work.
10. Reduce external scaffolding.
11. Observe what persists.
12. Adjust the system rather than blaming the person.

The governing principle is:

Behaviour is not merely a property of the individual. It is evidence about the system the individual is acting inside.
